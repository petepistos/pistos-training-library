Case Study: Ubiquiti — $46 Million Lost to a Fake Email
Narration playing…
— What Happened
2015 — a finance employee wired $46.7M to criminal accounts
The wire transfer that should not have happened
In 2015, Ubiquiti Networks — a networking equipment manufacturer — lost $46.7 million in a business email compromise (BEC) attack. A Ubiquiti finance employee received emails appearing to come from a company executive and from Ubiquiti's outside legal counsel, instructing the employee to initiate wire transfers to bank accounts as part of a confidential acquisition transaction. The employee complied, initiating multiple wire transfers totaling $46.7 million to accounts in Hong Kong, Russia, Hungary, China, and Poland. The transfers went unquestioned. The accounts were controlled by criminals. Ubiquiti discovered the fraud only after the fact.
What BEC is and why it works
Business email compromise is fraud conducted through email impersonation. The attacker does not need to hack anything — they need to be convincing. In the Ubiquiti attack, the attackers impersonated senior executives and legal counsel with sufficient credibility to cause a finance employee to initiate tens of millions of dollars in wire transfers. The psychology that makes BEC work: authority (the request appears to come from the CEO or senior management), urgency (the transaction is time-sensitive), secrecy (the employee is asked to keep the transaction confidential, preventing consultation with colleagues), and legitimacy (the context — an acquisition — is a plausible reason for unusual wire transfers).
Ubiquiti was not uniquely vulnerable
The Ubiquiti BEC attack was not the result of any unusual technical vulnerability or organizational dysfunction. Finance employees who process wire transfers are the targets of BEC attacks at companies of every size, every industry, and every level of security sophistication. BEC is the most costly cybercrime category tracked by the FBI — the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) has reported annual BEC losses exceeding $2 billion every year since 2020. Ubiquiti is among the most prominent cases because of the magnitude of the loss and the company's subsequent disclosure, but the attack pattern — fake executive email, wire transfer instruction, bank account in foreign jurisdiction — has been repeated thousands of times against organizations worldwide.
How much was recovered
Ubiquiti worked with law enforcement and financial regulators to attempt to recover the transferred funds. Of the $46.7 million transferred, approximately $8.1 million was recovered through freezing of accounts and international law enforcement coordination. The remaining $38.6 million was unrecovered — transferred through a chain of accounts in multiple jurisdictions, converted to other assets, or simply moved beyond the reach of the recovery effort. The $38.6 million permanent loss was disclosed in Ubiquiti's SEC filings, making it one of the largest publicly disclosed BEC losses on record at the time.
— How It Happened
Impersonation, urgency, and a wire transfer without verification
Step 1: Research and impersonation setup
Before sending a single email, the attackers researched Ubiquiti. They identified the company's finance personnel — the people authorized to initiate wire transfers. They identified senior executives whose authority would be sufficient to authorize large transfers. They identified outside legal counsel whose involvement would add legitimacy to an unusual transaction. This research is conducted through LinkedIn, corporate websites, press releases, and public filings — all publicly available information. The attackers then established email addresses that convincingly impersonated these identities, either by registering look-alike domains (ubiquti.com instead of ubnt.com) or by spoofing email headers to display legitimate-looking sender information.
Step 2: The initial email — authority + urgency + secrecy
The finance employee received an email appearing to come from a senior executive, describing a confidential acquisition and requesting wire transfers to specific bank accounts. The email included several social engineering elements: the instruction came from apparent authority (the CEO or CFO), the transaction was described as time-sensitive, the employee was instructed to keep the transaction confidential — which prevented them from verifying with colleagues, and the context (acquisition) was plausible for a technology company and explained why the accounts were in foreign jurisdictions.
Step 3: Emails from 'legal counsel' provided reinforcement
The attackers followed up with emails appearing to come from outside legal counsel, providing bank account details and additional instructions. The involvement of a second authority figure — legal counsel — provided reinforcement that the transaction was legitimate and properly authorized. The multi-actor impersonation is a BEC sophistication technique: it is harder to dismiss a request that appears to have been authorized by multiple senior people than one that appears to come from a single source. The finance employee, presented with consistent instructions from apparent executive authority and legal counsel, initiated the transfers.
Step 4: Multiple transfers before discovery
The fraud was not discovered after the first transfer — multiple transfers were made before anyone identified the pattern as fraudulent. Wire transfers, once executed, are extremely difficult to reverse: the funds move almost immediately to the recipient bank, and recovery depends on the recipient bank freezing the account before the funds are withdrawn or transferred again. Each successful transfer emboldened the attackers to request additional transfers. By the time the fraud was identified, $46.7 million had been wired across multiple jurisdictions, making recovery exponentially more difficult.
— Root Cause
No verification process for large wire transfers
Failure 1: No out-of-band verification for large transfers
The single most impactful missing control was a verification process: for any wire transfer above a defined threshold, the finance employee must verbally confirm the instruction with the apparent requestor by calling a known phone number — not a number provided in the request email. A simple phone call to the CFO using the number on file would have immediately revealed that the CFO had not sent the email. Out-of-band verification is called 'out-of-band' because it uses a different communication channel than the one that may be compromised: if the email is fraudulent, a phone call to a known number provides independent verification that the email channel cannot undermine.
Failure 2: No dual-control requirement for large transfers
Best practice for large wire transfers requires dual control — two authorized individuals must approve the transaction before it is executed. If the Ubiquiti finance employee was the only person required to authorize the transfers, a single point of social engineering pressure was sufficient to execute $46.7 million in fraudulent transfers. A dual-control requirement means the attacker must simultaneously deceive two independent employees — significantly harder than deceiving one. Most banking relationships support dual-control requirements for wire transfers above defined thresholds; organizations must specifically configure and enforce this control.
Failure 3: Confidentiality instruction prevented verification
The 'keep this confidential' instruction in the fraudulent email was a deliberate social engineering technique to prevent the target from consulting with colleagues. A finance employee who discusses an unusual wire transfer request with a manager, a compliance officer, or the actual executive being impersonated has multiple opportunities to identify the fraud before executing it. Organizations should establish explicitly that no financial transaction — regardless of who appears to be requesting it — should be kept secret from the employee's manager or from the finance team's normal approval process. Confidentiality instructions in wire transfer requests are a BEC red flag.
Failure 4: Email authentication did not prevent impersonation
The attackers were able to send emails that appeared to come from Ubiquiti executives either because Ubiquiti's email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) was not fully configured, or because the attackers used look-alike domains that were close enough to be convincing. DMARC configured to reject policy prevents spoofing of the organization's own domain — an attacker cannot send an email that appears to come from [email protected] if DMARC p=reject is configured. Look-alike domain attacks ([email protected]) are harder to prevent technically and require training employees to check email addresses carefully.
— The Loss
$46.7 million wired, $38.6 million never recovered
The financial loss: $38.6 million permanent
Of the $46.7 million transferred, only $8.1 million was recovered through law enforcement and international account freezing. The remaining $38.6 million was permanently lost — an amount that was significant relative to Ubiquiti's size as a company. The loss was disclosed in Ubiquiti's SEC filings as a material adverse event. The SEC disclosure requirement for material losses reflects the regulatory principle that investors have a right to know about events that materially affect a public company's financial position. The disclosure made the Ubiquiti BEC one of the most prominent cases in the FBI's BEC case catalog.
Reputational impact: investor and market response
The SEC disclosure of a $46.7 million wire fraud loss caused Ubiquiti's stock price to decline significantly. Investors' reaction reflected concerns about the company's internal financial controls, specifically that a single finance employee could authorize nearly $47 million in wire transfers based on email instructions alone, without verification or dual-control approval. The reputational impact extended beyond the immediate stock price decline: the case became a widely cited example of BEC risk, and Ubiquiti's name has appeared in security awareness training materials ever since as a cautionary case study.
The recovery effort: law enforcement and international coordination
The recovery of $8.1 million was achieved through rapid reporting to law enforcement and coordination with foreign banking regulators to freeze accounts before funds were fully dispersed. The recovery rate — approximately 17% of the total — reflects both the speed of the recovery effort and the structural challenge of recovering wire transfers from accounts in multiple foreign jurisdictions. Wire transfers are designed to be irreversible; the financial system's recovery mechanisms depend on speed (freezing accounts before funds move again) and international legal cooperation (getting foreign banks and regulators to cooperate). Both become harder with time and with geographic spread across multiple jurisdictions.
BEC as the leading cybercrime loss category
Ubiquiti's $46.7 million loss is notable but not exceptional in the context of the overall BEC problem. The FBI's IC3 has tracked BEC losses totaling over $50 billion since 2013. Single BEC incidents in excess of $100 million have been reported. The manufacturing, real estate, and financial services sectors are the most targeted. BEC attacks require no malware, no technical expertise, and no network access — they require only research, convincing impersonation, and a target who lacks the verification controls to identify fraudulent instructions. The FBI consistently identifies BEC as the highest-dollar cybercrime category, exceeding ransomware losses by a wide margin.
— Lessons Learned
What Ubiquiti teaches about social engineering and financial controls
Out-of-band verbal verification is mandatory for large wire transfers
Any wire transfer above a defined threshold must be verbally confirmed by calling the apparent requestor at a known phone number — not a number provided in the request. This single control defeats BEC. The call takes two minutes. The $46.7 million loss would not have happened.
Dual control: two independent approvals for every large transfer
No single employee should be able to authorize a large wire transfer alone. Two independent approvals — using separate communication channels — make simultaneous deception of both approvers exponentially harder. Configure dual-control requirements with your bank.
Confidentiality instructions in financial requests are a red flag
No legitimate executive will instruct you to keep a financial transaction secret from your manager or the normal approval process. Train this as an explicit BEC indicator. Any confidentiality instruction in a wire transfer request should trigger immediate escalation.
DMARC p=reject prevents your domain from being spoofed
Configure DMARC at p=reject so attackers cannot send email appearing to come from your domain. Train employees to check the full email address of any wire transfer instruction. Look-alike domains (ubiquti.com) require visual inspection to catch.
