— What Happened
May 2021 — one stolen password shut down the East Coast fuel supply
A single password, a national emergency
On May 7, 2021, Colonial Pipeline shut down operations. The cause was a ransomware attack by a criminal group called DarkSide. Colonial paid $4.4 million in cryptocurrency ransom within hours. Six days later, pipeline operations resumed. The shutdown caused fuel shortages across 17 states, long lines at gas stations, panic buying, and a six-cent-per-gallon spike in the national gasoline price. President Biden declared a state of emergency. The cause: a single compromised VPN password with no MFA protecting it.
How the password was obtained
DarkSide gained access using a username and password for a legacy VPN account. The credential had been exposed in a separate, earlier data breach of a different service and was available in dark web breach data dumps. An employee had reused the same password across multiple services. Colonial had not enabled multi-factor authentication on this VPN account. With the correct username and password, DarkSide logged into Colonial's network just as a legitimate user would have done.
What DarkSide did once inside
DarkSide moved laterally through Colonial's IT network, conducting reconnaissance and staging the attack. Before deploying ransomware, the group exfiltrated approximately 100 gigabytes of data as a double extortion tactic: pay the ransom or the data will be published publicly. On May 7, DarkSide deployed ransomware across Colonial's IT systems. Colonial shut down the pipeline as a precaution because it was uncertain whether the OT systems controlling the physical pipeline had been affected.
The shutdown and its consequences
Colonial's decision to preemptively shut down the pipeline was made out of concern for safety and operational integrity. The IT and OT networks were connected enough that the company could not confirm OT systems were unaffected. The six-day shutdown was the most significant disruption to US fuel supply since Hurricane Katrina. Colonial paid the ransom of 75 Bitcoin within hours of the attack. The DOJ later recovered approximately $2.3 million by tracing and seizing the cryptocurrency wallet.
— How It Happened
Reused password to ransomware to national emergency
Step 1: Credential found in a dark web breach dump
DarkSide searched dark web credential databases for valid credentials belonging to Colonial's systems or employees. They found a username and password for a VPN account — a credential exposed in a prior, unrelated data breach where the employee had used the same password. When that service was breached and the credentials were stolen, the same password worked for Colonial's VPN.
Step 2: VPN login with no MFA to stop it
With the correct username and password, DarkSide authenticated to Colonial's VPN. The account had no multi-factor authentication. MFA would have required a code from an authenticator app or push notification to a registered device that DarkSide could not have provided. The stolen password alone was sufficient. The attacker was in.
Step 3: Lateral movement and reconnaissance
Once inside the VPN, DarkSide moved laterally through Colonial's IT network. They conducted reconnaissance, identified valuable data targets, and staged the 100 gigabytes of data for exfiltration. Organizations that detect and respond to the initial access during this reconnaissance window can stop the ransomware before it deploys.
Step 4: Exfiltration, ransomware, and shutdown
DarkSide exfiltrated 100 gigabytes before deploying the ransomware. The ransomware encrypted systems across Colonial's IT network. Colonial, unable to confirm whether OT systems were affected, shut down the pipeline as a precaution. The shutdown caused the national fuel crisis. Colonial paid $4.4 million in ransom. The DOJ subsequently recovered approximately $2.3 million by seizing the cryptocurrency wallet.
— Root Cause
Simple failures that caused a national security incident
Failure 1: No MFA on VPN
The single most consequential control failure was the absence of multi-factor authentication on Colonial's VPN. MFA makes stolen credentials insufficient: knowing the username and password is not enough, the attacker must also have the second factor tied to a registered device. The stolen credential was usable because there was no second factor. MFA on VPN is required by NY DFS 23 NYCRR 500.12 for all covered entities — Colonial's failure is exactly the scenario that requirement is designed to prevent.
Failure 2: Password reuse across services
The credential DarkSide used was not obtained by attacking Colonial. It came from a different breach of a different service where the employee reused the same password. Password reuse allows a breach of one service to compromise every other service where the same credential is used. Unique passwords for every account — enforced through a password manager — is the control that breaks this chain and prevents one breach from enabling another.
Failure 3: No credential monitoring or dark web scanning
The stolen Colonial credential was available on dark web markets before the DarkSide attack. Colonial was not monitoring for exposed credentials. Dark web credential monitoring checks whether organizational email addresses appear in known breach databases and alerts when they do. This would have flagged the exposure and prompted a password reset before attackers used it. The monitoring is available from commercial services at modest cost.
Failure 4: IT/OT connectivity required pipeline shutdown
The ransomware affected Colonial's IT network. The pipeline control systems may not have been directly compromised, but Colonial could not confirm OT system integrity given the connectivity between IT and OT networks. Proper IT/OT segmentation would have allowed Colonial to verify OT systems were unaffected with certainty, potentially avoiding the shutdown or enabling faster resumption of operations.
— The Loss
$4.4M ransom, a national fuel crisis, and new mandatory regulations
Financial: $4.4M ransom paid, mostly recovered
Colonial paid 75 Bitcoin in cryptocurrency ransom to DarkSide within hours of the attack. Payment was made quickly because Colonial determined that the fastest path to restoring operations was the decryption key. In June 2021, the Department of Justice announced recovery of approximately $2.3 million by tracking and seizing the cryptocurrency wallet. The recovery demonstrated that Bitcoin ransoms are not as anonymous as criminals assumed.
National impact: fuel shortage across 17 states
The six-day shutdown caused fuel shortages across the southeastern United States affecting 17 states. Gas stations ran out of fuel. Average gasoline prices rose approximately six cents per gallon — the largest one-week increase in years. Multiple states declared states of emergency and waived transportation regulations to allow more fuel movement by truck. Airlines were affected as jet fuel availability at East Coast airports tightened.
Regulatory aftermath: mandatory TSA security directives
Following the attack, the TSA issued mandatory security directives to US pipeline operators for the first time. The directives required: reporting cybersecurity incidents to CISA within 12 hours, a 24/7 cybersecurity coordinator, a review of cybersecurity practices, and implementation of specific controls including access controls and monitoring. A second directive in July 2021 specifically mandated MFA, network segmentation, and related controls.
Precedent: Critical infrastructure cybersecurity as national policy
The Colonial attack was a primary catalyst for President Biden's Executive Order on Improving the Nation's Cybersecurity. The order established new standards for federal systems and critical infrastructure, accelerated zero trust adoption in the federal government, and established new government-private sector information sharing requirements. DarkSide disbanded after the attack, apparently due to US government pressure — demonstrating that sufficiently high-profile attacks can produce consequences for the attackers.
— Lessons Learned
What Colonial Pipeline teaches about passwords and MFA
MFA on VPN is non-negotiable for remote access
A single stolen password should never be sufficient to access an organization's network through VPN. MFA makes stolen credentials insufficient for access. MFA on VPN is required by NY DFS 23 NYCRR 500.12 and is the single most impactful control that would have prevented the Colonial Pipeline attack entirely.
Password reuse is how one breach becomes many breaches
When an employee reuses a password across multiple services, a breach of any one service gives attackers a credential that works everywhere the password is reused. Unique passwords for every account, enforced through a password manager, breaks this chain. A password manager makes it practical to have a unique, complex password for every service without requiring memorization.
Dark web credential monitoring is cheap and effective
Stolen credentials appear on dark web marketplaces before attackers use them. Credential monitoring services check whether organizational email addresses and passwords appear in known breach databases and alert when they do. This enables proactive password resets before the credential is weaponized. This control would have directly and specifically prevented the Colonial Pipeline breach.
IT/OT segmentation means a cyber incident stays cyber
The Colonial Pipeline shutdown was caused not by ransomware reaching the pipeline controls but by uncertainty about whether it had. Proper IT/OT segmentation — no network path from IT systems to OT systems — means an IT breach cannot reach OT systems, and the organization can verify this with certainty. That certainty is operationally critical for any organization running physical infrastructure.
