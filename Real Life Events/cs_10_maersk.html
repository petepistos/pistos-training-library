— What Happened
June 2017 — 45,000 PCs and 4,000 servers wiped in hours
The most destructive malware attack in history
On June 27, 2017, Maersk — the world's largest shipping company, handling roughly 20% of global seaborne trade — was hit by NotPetya, a cyberweapon disguised as ransomware. NotPetya did not encrypt files for ransom; it overwrote the master boot records of every system it reached, making them permanently unbootable. Within hours, 45,000 PCs, 4,000 servers, and 2,500 applications across Maersk's global operations were destroyed. Maersk's global terminal network — the computerized systems that track and direct the movement of shipping containers — went dark. Ports across the world could not operate. Ships could not load or unload. The supply chain that moves a significant fraction of global trade stopped.
How it spread so fast
NotPetya entered Maersk's network through MEDoc, Ukrainian accounting software that Maersk was required to use for Ukrainian tax compliance. MEDoc had been compromised by Russian intelligence; its automatic update mechanism was weaponized to deliver NotPetya to all MEDoc users simultaneously. Once inside a network, NotPetya used two propagation techniques: EternalBlue (an NSA exploit leaked by the Shadow Brokers) to spread through unpatched SMB vulnerabilities, and credential harvesting via Mimikatz to use stolen credentials for lateral movement to systems that were patched. NotPetya could infect any machine reachable from an infected machine — patching was necessary but not sufficient to stop it.
Operations: 10 days without computers
Maersk operated its global shipping empire with no functioning IT systems for approximately 10 days. Terminals reverted to manual operations — pen and paper, walkie-talkies, and improvised spreadsheets shared on personal WhatsApp groups. Maersk employees flew to ports around the world to direct operations manually. The company could not issue new bills of lading, could not update container tracking systems, and could not process customs documentation electronically. An estimated 300,000 shipping containers were stranded globally. The port of Los Angeles operated at reduced capacity. Importers and exporters worldwide experienced delivery delays as a knock-on effect.
Recovery: reinstalling from a single domain controller
Maersk's recovery required reinstalling the entire global IT infrastructure — every PC, server, and application — from scratch. This was possible because Maersk's backup systems, which were connected to the main network, had also been destroyed. Recovery depended on a single domain controller in Ghana that happened to be offline during the attack due to a power outage. That single machine, which retained the Active Directory database with the identities and configurations needed to rebuild the network, was physically flown to Maersk's headquarters in Copenhagen and used as the starting point for rebuilding the entire global IT infrastructure. Without that one offline machine, recovery would have been significantly more complex.
— How It Happened
From a software update to global destruction in hours
NotPetya was a cyberweapon, not ransomware
NotPetya displayed a ransom message, but it was not designed to recover files. The encryption mechanism for the master boot record was intentionally implemented without a working decryption mechanism — there was no key, no recovery path, and no intent to provide one. The ransom message was cover. NotPetya was a cyberweapon deployed by Russia's GRU military intelligence unit to cause maximum damage to Ukrainian infrastructure during the ongoing conflict in Ukraine. Maersk and the other non-Ukrainian companies that were destroyed were collateral damage — victims of a nation-state weapon that escaped its intended geographic scope through the global connectivity of supply chains.
EternalBlue: the NSA exploit that became a weapon
EternalBlue was an exploit developed by the NSA targeting a vulnerability in the Windows SMB protocol. The exploit was stolen from the NSA by a group calling itself the Shadow Brokers and published on the internet in April 2017 — approximately two months before NotPetya. Microsoft had released a patch (MS17-010) in March 2017, but organizations that had not applied the patch remained vulnerable. NotPetya used EternalBlue to spread through any SMB-accessible system that lacked the patch. Organizations that had patched all systems were not immune — NotPetya's Mimikatz component harvested administrative credentials from memory and used them to spread to patched systems through legitimate administrative protocols.
Why Maersk could not stop it
NotPetya's dual propagation mechanism — EternalBlue for unpatched systems, Mimikatz for credential reuse on patched systems — meant that no single control could stop it. Network segmentation that would have prevented lateral movement was absent; Maersk's global network was interconnected in ways that allowed NotPetya to reach every system reachable from any infected system. Endpoint security did not detect NotPetya before it executed on many systems. The MEDoc supply chain entry point was outside Maersk's security perimeter — the attack arrived as a legitimate software update from a trusted vendor. Once NotPetya reached a system with domain admin credentials in memory, it could spread to any system in the domain.
The backup failure: connected backups are not backups
Maersk's disaster recovery planning was based on the assumption that backups would survive an attack on the primary systems. NotPetya disproved that assumption. Backup systems that are connected to the network can be reached by malware that propagates through the network. Backups that are online and accessible are destroyed by wiper malware at the same time as primary systems. The backup failure meant that recovery required rebuilding from scratch rather than restoring from backup. The Ghana domain controller — offline by accident — demonstrated what offline backups would have provided by design: a recovery point that the malware could not reach.
— Root Cause
Supply chain entry, flat network, and connected backups
Failure 1: Supply chain software was trusted without verification
Maersk had no way to verify that the MEDoc update was clean — it arrived through MEDoc's normal update channel, signed as a legitimate update. Supply chain attacks through software updates are among the most difficult attacks to defend against because the attack arrives through a trusted channel. Mitigations: verify software integrity through independent channels where possible, monitor update behavior for anomalies, and segment systems that run jurisdiction-specific software from the broader network so that a compromised update cannot spread beyond the contained zone.
Failure 2: Network segmentation was absent
NotPetya spread from Maersk's Ukrainian MEDoc systems to its global network because all systems were interconnected. A segmented network — where the Ukrainian accounting systems were in a separate zone with limited connectivity to the global network — would have contained NotPetya to that zone. The 45,000 machines destroyed could have been a much smaller number if the propagation path had been limited by architecture. Network segmentation is the control that limits what can be reached from any compromised system; without it, any compromise becomes a global compromise.
Failure 3: Connected backups provided no recovery point
Disaster recovery planning that assumes an attacker cannot reach backup systems is planning for a different attack than NotPetya represents. Wiper malware that propagates through a network will reach connected backup systems. The backup strategy for any organization must include offline backups — backups that are not connected to the network at the time of the attack. The Ghana domain controller demonstrated the principle: the one machine that was offline was the one machine that survived. Air-gapped or offline backups are not anachronistic; they are essential protection against wiper malware.
Failure 4: Unpatched systems provided the initial propagation vector
EternalBlue exploited a Windows SMB vulnerability for which Microsoft had released a patch two months before NotPetya. Organizations that had applied MS17-010 to all systems were not immune — Mimikatz handled credential reuse on patched systems — but the unpatched systems amplified NotPetya's initial spread. Vulnerability management — systematic identification and remediation of known vulnerabilities within defined timelines — is a foundational control. The EternalBlue exploit was public knowledge for two months before NotPetya; organizations that applied the patch promptly limited their exposure to the initial propagation vector, even if not to the credential-based spread.
— The Loss
$300 million and ten days without computers
Direct cost: $300 million to Maersk
Maersk reported a net impact of approximately $300 million from the NotPetya attack, covering the cost of rebuilding 45,000 PCs and 4,000 servers, reinstalling 2,500 applications, recovering operational data, and managing the 10-day operational disruption. The recovery required reinstalling the entire global IT infrastructure — a project that took approximately 10 days for the core systems and weeks for full restoration. Maersk's initial disclosure of the impact understated the severity; subsequent insurance claims and investor disclosures provided a fuller picture of the financial damage. Maersk's cyber insurance coverage provided partial recovery, but the magnitude of the loss substantially exceeded coverage limits.
Operational disruption: 20% of global shipping affected
Maersk handles approximately 20% of global seaborne containerized trade through its terminal network. When those terminals went dark, approximately one-fifth of global container shipping capacity was disrupted simultaneously. The operational disruption affected every industry that relies on containerized shipping — which is to say, every industry that manufactures or trades physical goods. Importers and exporters worldwide experienced delays, missing deliveries, and supply chain disruptions as a cascade effect of Maersk's system failure. The port of New York and New Jersey reported significant disruption; ports in Los Angeles, Rotterdam, and Mumbai were similarly affected.
Global collateral damage: $10 billion+ across all victims
NotPetya was not targeted at Maersk specifically. The same weapon destroyed systems at FedEx/TNT ($400M loss), pharmaceutical company Merck ($870M loss), construction firm Saint-Gobain ($384M loss), and hundreds of other organizations globally. The U.S. government attributed NotPetya to Russia's GRU and estimated total global damages at over $10 billion — making it the most destructive cyberattack in history. Merck's insurance claim for losses attributable to NotPetya was initially denied by its insurer on the grounds that NotPetya was an act of war; the resulting litigation established important precedents about cyber insurance coverage for nation-state attacks.
Recovery architecture: rebuilding from a single domain controller
The technical story of Maersk's recovery has become a case study in disaster recovery. The company had to reinstall 45,000 PCs and 4,000 servers. It did so by sourcing new hardware globally — purchasing every available laptop from retail stores in multiple countries — and rebuilding the Active Directory domain from the single offline domain controller in Ghana. Within 10 days, Maersk had restored core operations. The recovery demonstrated extraordinary organizational resilience under catastrophic conditions, but it also demonstrated that the outcome depended on a single point of luck: one offline machine in Ghana. A recovery plan that depends on luck is not a plan.
— Lessons Learned
What NotPetya and Maersk teach about resilience
Offline backups are not optional — they are the recovery plan
NotPetya destroyed connected backups along with primary systems. The Ghana domain controller — offline by accident — made recovery possible. Every organization needs offline or air-gapped backups that cannot be reached by malware that propagates through the network. Test restoration annually.
Network segmentation limits propagation to segments
Maersk's globally interconnected network allowed NotPetya to reach every system. Proper segmentation — particularly isolating jurisdiction-specific software like MEDoc in a contained zone — would have limited destruction to that zone. Flat networks mean any compromise becomes a total compromise.
Supply chain software is your attack surface
Maersk was required by Ukrainian law to run MEDoc. That compliance requirement became the weapon's delivery mechanism. Supply chain software — accounting systems, industry-specific tools, vendor portals — runs with trust that attackers exploit. Segment supply chain software. Monitor update behavior. Verify integrity.
Patch known vulnerabilities within defined timelines
EternalBlue had a patch available two months before NotPetya. Unpatched systems amplified initial propagation. Mimikatz covered patched systems, but limiting EternalBlue's reach limited overall destruction. Vulnerability management with defined patch timelines is a foundational control — not optional.
