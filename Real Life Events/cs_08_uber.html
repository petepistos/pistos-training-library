Case Study: Uber — The MFA Fatigue Attack
Narration playing…
— What Happened
September 2022 — an 18-year-old walked into Uber's network
The breach
In September 2022, an 18-year-old attacker gained access to Uber's internal network, Slack workspace, HackerOne vulnerability reports, and cloud infrastructure. The attacker announced the breach by posting messages in Uber's own Slack channels, reaching Uber employees directly. The attacker had access to Uber's most sensitive internal systems for several hours before Uber detected the intrusion and began shutting down systems. The method used to bypass Uber's multi-factor authentication was not a technical exploit — it was social engineering combined with a technique called MFA fatigue, in which the victim is overwhelmed with authentication requests until they approve one to make the notifications stop.
What was accessed
The attacker accessed Uber's internal Slack workspace and sent messages to employees. They accessed Uber's HackerOne account, which contained confidential vulnerability reports submitted by security researchers — reports describing unpatched vulnerabilities in Uber's systems. They accessed Uber's Amazon Web Services console, Google Cloud Platform console, and internal administrative tools. They accessed Uber's code repositories and internal wikis. Screenshots posted by the attacker showed access to Uber's most sensitive internal systems. The HackerOne access was particularly damaging: vulnerability reports are confidential until patches are deployed, and an attacker with access to unpatched vulnerability reports has a roadmap of exploitable weaknesses.
Who the attacker was
The attacker was an 18-year-old who later claimed affiliation with the Lapsus$ group, a loosely organized hacking collective responsible for breaches at Microsoft, Samsung, Nvidia, and Okta in the preceding months. The attacker communicated openly with security researchers during the breach, describing their access and methods in real time. The teenager reportedly spent approximately $20 buying the Uber employee's credentials on the dark web and invested a few hours in the social engineering attack. The breach required no sophisticated technical capability — it required credentials that were available for purchase, a phone capable of sending WhatsApp messages, and patience.
How Uber responded
Uber took several internal systems offline after detecting the breach, including Slack and internal tools. The company engaged law enforcement and a digital forensics firm. Uber's public statement attributed the breach to the Lapsus$ group and described it as a social engineering attack. The company reported that no customer data or payment information was accessed, though the attacker's access to internal systems and vulnerability reports represented a significant security incident regardless of customer data exposure. The breach prompted a review of Uber's MFA implementation and third-party access controls.
— How It Happened
From purchased credentials to full internal access in hours
Step 1: Buy credentials on the dark web
The attacker purchased Uber employee credentials from a dark web marketplace for approximately $20. Credential markets sell username and password combinations harvested from previous breaches, malware campaigns, and phishing attacks. The credentials were valid because the Uber employee used the same password across multiple services — a practice that allows credential stuffing attacks to succeed when any service where the password was used is breached. The $20 credential purchase was the entire technical investment required to initiate the attack.
Step 2: MFA fatigue — spam the employee until they approve
The credentials alone were insufficient because Uber required MFA. The attacker used the MFA fatigue technique: they attempted to log in repeatedly using the stolen credentials, triggering push notification MFA requests to the employee's phone. Each failed login generated another push notification. The employee received dozens of MFA requests over several hours. The attacker then contacted the employee directly on WhatsApp, claiming to be Uber IT support, and told the employee that the MFA requests would stop if they approved one. The employee approved the request. This single approval gave the attacker authenticated access to Uber's network as a legitimate user.
Step 3: Privilege escalation via a PowerShell script
Once inside Uber's network as an authenticated user, the attacker found a shared network drive containing PowerShell scripts. One of those scripts contained hardcoded administrator credentials in plaintext. The attacker used those credentials to access Uber's privileged account management system, which then provided access to all of Uber's cloud infrastructure: AWS, GCP, and internal administrative tools. A single hardcoded password in a script — a configuration management failure entirely separate from the MFA attack — turned authenticated user access into full administrative access across Uber's entire infrastructure.
Step 4: Full access, open communication
With administrative credentials to Uber's cloud infrastructure, the attacker had access to virtually every internal system. They accessed Slack, HackerOne, code repositories, and administrative dashboards. Rather than conducting covert operations, the attacker announced the breach by posting in Uber's Slack. Security researchers who saw the messages initially assumed it was a joke. The attacker communicated openly with researchers, sharing screenshots of their access. The open communication made the breach immediately visible but also provided a public record of the extent of access the attacker had achieved.
— Root Cause
Three failures that turned a $20 purchase into a full breach
Failure 1: MFA method susceptible to fatigue attacks
Uber used push notification MFA — the method where the user receives a notification on their phone and taps 'Approve' or 'Deny.' Push notification MFA is convenient and is better than no MFA. But it is vulnerable to MFA fatigue attacks, in which the attacker triggers enough requests that the user approves one to stop the notifications. It is also vulnerable to social engineering, as demonstrated when the attacker contacted the employee directly and impersonated IT support. Push notification MFA does not verify that the user is intentionally approving a login they initiated — it only verifies that someone with the user's phone tapped Approve. Number matching (requiring the user to match a number displayed during the login to a number in the push notification) and phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2/hardware keys) both prevent this attack.
Failure 2: Hardcoded credentials in a shared script
The privilege escalation from ordinary user to full administrator was enabled by a completely separate failure: administrator credentials hardcoded in plaintext in a PowerShell script stored on a shared network drive. This is a configuration management failure that is unrelated to MFA. Credentials should never be hardcoded in scripts. Scripts that require credentials should use secrets management systems (AWS Secrets Manager, Azure Key Vault, HashiCorp Vault) that provide credentials at runtime without storing them in the script itself. The shared network drive where the script was stored amplified the exposure: any authenticated user who could access the drive could read the script and extract the credentials.
Failure 3: Insufficient access to HackerOne and vulnerability data
Uber's HackerOne account — which contained confidential reports of unpatched vulnerabilities — was accessible with the same credentials used for internal systems. Vulnerability reports are among the most sensitive data in a security program: they provide an adversary with a roadmap of exploitable weaknesses that have been identified but not yet patched. Access to vulnerability management systems should be restricted to the security team members who need it, should require additional authentication factors, and should be logged and monitored for unusual access patterns. The attacker's access to HackerOne was as damaging as their access to the cloud infrastructure, because it exposed information about vulnerabilities that had not yet been remediated.
The social engineering success
The employee who approved the MFA request responded to pressure from someone claiming to be IT support. This reflects the challenge of training humans to resist social engineering: the employee was trying to resolve what appeared to be a persistent technical problem, and a helpful-seeming authority figure provided a simple solution. Organizations that use push notification MFA must train employees that IT support will never ask them to approve an MFA request they did not initiate, and that any such request should be treated as a potential attack and reported immediately. The technical solution is to eliminate the attack vector entirely by moving to phishing-resistant MFA that does not rely on user judgment.
— The Loss
Reputational, strategic, and regulatory consequences
Immediate impact: systems offline and internal exposure
Uber took multiple internal systems offline, including Slack, as it investigated and contained the breach. The operational disruption during the containment period affected internal communications and workflows. The attacker's access to HackerOne created an ongoing security risk: if the attacker copied vulnerability reports before access was terminated, those reports could be used to develop exploits for unpatched vulnerabilities even after the immediate breach was contained. Uber's security team had to treat all vulnerabilities in open HackerOne reports as potentially known to an adversary and prioritize their remediation accordingly.
Reputational damage and employee trust
The attacker's announcement of the breach through Uber's own Slack system — visible to Uber employees — created an unusual form of reputational damage: internal. Employees saw real-time evidence that an outsider had complete access to their communications environment. The breach occurred less than a year after Uber's former CISO, Joe Sullivan, was convicted of obstruction of justice for his handling of a 2016 Uber data breach — a conviction that raised the stakes of any new breach from a legal and regulatory perspective. The combination of the Sullivan conviction and the 2022 breach made Uber's security history a defining feature of its corporate narrative.
Regulatory scrutiny: the Sullivan conviction context
The timing of the 2022 breach — coming after the Sullivan conviction — intensified regulatory scrutiny. The Sullivan case established that concealing a breach from regulators could result in criminal liability for the security executive responsible. The 2022 breach occurred in this heightened accountability environment. Uber's transparent disclosure and cooperation with law enforcement reflected the lesson the Sullivan case had taught the industry: the cover-up creates greater liability than the breach. The regulatory impact of the 2022 breach was moderated by Uber's transparent response, but the context of the company's recent security history meant that every disclosure decision was made against a backdrop of exceptional scrutiny.
Industry impact: MFA fatigue becomes a standard attack category
The Uber breach, combined with other high-profile Lapsus$ attacks using similar techniques, elevated MFA fatigue from an obscure attack technique to a standard category in security awareness training and threat assessments. CISA and other government security agencies issued guidance on MFA fatigue attacks following the wave of Lapsus$ incidents. Organizations across industries reviewed their MFA implementations to assess susceptibility to fatigue attacks. The breach demonstrated that MFA — long considered a near-sufficient control against credential-based attacks — could be defeated through social engineering without any technical exploit. This drove adoption of number matching, additional context in push notifications, and phishing-resistant MFA methods.
— Lessons Learned
What Uber teaches every organization about MFA
Push notification MFA is vulnerable to fatigue — move to phishing-resistant MFA
Number matching is the minimum upgrade; FIDO2/hardware keys are the standard for privileged access. The attack that cost Uber $20 in credentials and a few hours of WhatsApp messages would have failed against a FIDO2 hardware key — the attacker cannot remotely approve a hardware key authentication.
Credentials must never be hardcoded in scripts
The privilege escalation from user to administrator required only finding a script on a shared drive. Secrets management systems eliminate this risk entirely. Every script that contains a hardcoded credential is a ticking time bomb waiting for the first authenticated user to look at it.
Sensitive security systems need additional access controls
Vulnerability reports, security tooling, and administrative consoles must be restricted beyond standard user credentials. Separate authentication, strict role-based access, and access logging provide defense in depth for the systems whose compromise creates the most leverage for an attacker.
Social engineering defeats technical controls — train the humans
Employees must know that IT support will never ask them to approve an MFA request they did not initiate. Any such request is an attack. The human response to social engineering pressure is the last line of defense when technical controls are insufficient — make sure employees know what to do.
