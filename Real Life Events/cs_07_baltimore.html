— What Happened
May 2019 — ransomware shut down the government of a major US city
A city government brought to its knees
On May 7, 2019, Baltimore's city government was hit by RobbinHood ransomware. Within hours, 13 of the city's 18 government departments were offline. Email was down. The city's online payment systems were inaccessible. Property records, water billing, parking tickets, and permit processing all stopped. City employees across departments reverted to pen and paper. Residents could not access city services. The attack affected approximately 600,000 people and paralyzed city operations for weeks. Attackers demanded approximately $76,000 in Bitcoin. Baltimore refused to pay.
What systems were affected
The ransomware spread rapidly across Baltimore's city network, encrypting systems across 13 departments. Among the most disruptive losses: the city's email system, which was down for weeks; the city's online payment portal, preventing residents from paying water bills and property taxes; the property records system, which halted real estate closings city-wide; the permitting system, blocking construction and business license applications; and the 911 dispatch support systems, which required manual backup procedures. The breadth of the outage reflected the flat, unsegmented nature of the city's network.
How the attack happened
The attackers used a combination of two techniques. The initial entry point was Remote Desktop Protocol exposed directly to the internet — a management port that should never be accessible from the public internet without a VPN. From that entry point, attackers used EternalBlue, an exploit targeting the Windows SMB vulnerability known as MS17-010. Microsoft had released a critical patch for this vulnerability in March 2017. Baltimore's systems had not been patched in the more than two years since that patch was released.
Baltimore's response: refuse to pay
Baltimore's mayor, Bernard Young, and city council declined to pay the $76,000 ransom. The decision was consistent with guidance from the FBI and cybersecurity professionals — paying ransoms funds future attacks and does not guarantee recovery. But the decision was not without cost. Baltimore spent more than $18 million on recovery: IT contractors, replacement hardware, software licenses, and the staff time required to rebuild systems that had to be reconstructed largely from scratch due to inadequate backups. The $76,000 ransom demand was replaced by an $18 million recovery bill.
— How It Happened
Internet-exposed RDP and an unpatched Windows vulnerability
Step 1: Internet-exposed RDP scanned and accessed
Remote Desktop Protocol is a Windows feature that allows remote administration of a computer. It is widely used by IT staff for remote management. When RDP is exposed directly to the internet — accessible from any IP address without requiring a VPN connection first — it becomes a target for automated scanning tools that attackers run continuously, looking for accessible RDP ports. Baltimore had RDP exposed on the public internet. The attackers identified it, gained access through exploitation or credential brute-forcing, and established their initial foothold.
Step 2: EternalBlue lateral movement
From the initial RDP foothold, the attackers deployed EternalBlue to move laterally through Baltimore's network. EternalBlue exploits a vulnerability in the Windows Server Message Block protocol. It was originally developed by the NSA and released publicly by the Shadow Brokers hacking group in April 2017. Microsoft had released patch MS17-010 in March 2017 — before the public release. Baltimore's systems had not been patched. The vulnerability allowed the ransomware to spread from machine to machine across Baltimore's network without requiring any user interaction.
Step 3: Spreading across the unpatched city network
EternalBlue spread the ransomware across Baltimore's flat, unsegmented network. Because all city departments were on the same network with no internal segmentation, the ransomware could move from its initial entry point to systems across 13 departments without crossing any internal security boundary. A segmented network would have contained the spread to the initial segment. The absence of segmentation allowed the ransomware to achieve city-wide impact.
Step 4: RobbinHood ransomware encrypts city systems
RobbinHood ransomware encrypted files across the systems it had reached, rendering them inaccessible. RobbinHood specifically targets network-connected drives and uses a driver-based technique to bypass antivirus software. The ransom note demanded approximately $76,000 in Bitcoin, threatening to increase the demand and permanently destroy files if payment was not made within 10 days. Baltimore chose not to pay and began the lengthy process of manually rebuilding its IT infrastructure.
— Root Cause
Four control failures that paralyzed 600,000 residents
Failure 1: RDP exposed directly to the internet
Remote Desktop Protocol should never be directly accessible from the public internet. RDP exposes administrative access to a Windows machine and has a history of vulnerabilities. When exposed on the public internet, RDP servers are continuously scanned and probed by automated tools. The correct architecture is to require VPN authentication before any RDP access — meaning attackers must first defeat VPN authentication before they can attempt any RDP attack. Baltimore had not applied this basic internet exposure reduction to its RDP infrastructure.
Failure 2: Critical patch MS17-010 not applied for two years
Microsoft released the patch for the EternalBlue vulnerability in March 2017 — weeks before WannaCry used the same vulnerability to cause global disruption in May 2017. Baltimore's systems were not patched with MS17-010 more than two years after the patch's release, despite WannaCry having dramatically demonstrated the consequences of not applying it. Effective patch management requires a defined process for critical patch application, with a maximum timeframe for deployment after release, and a tracking system that identifies unpatched systems.
Failure 3: No network segmentation — ransomware spread city-wide
Baltimore's city government operated all 13 affected departments on the same flat network. When the ransomware entered through RDP and spread via EternalBlue, there were no internal network boundaries to stop its expansion. Network segmentation — placing each department or function in its own network zone with firewall rules controlling communication between zones — would have contained the ransomware to the segment where it first appeared. The city-wide impact was a direct consequence of the city-wide network.
Failure 4: No tested offline backups for critical systems
Baltimore's recovery required rebuilding many systems essentially from scratch, at a cost that far exceeded the ransom demand. Adequate offline backups — copies of critical systems stored in locations the ransomware could not reach — would have made recovery a matter of restoring from backup rather than rebuilding from nothing. The absence of offline backup capability was the reason the recovery cost $18 million instead of the much smaller cost of restoring from backup and resuming operations.
— The Loss
$18M to recover from a $76K ransom demand
Financial: Refusing $76K ransom cost $18 million
The attackers demanded approximately $76,000 in Bitcoin. Baltimore's mayor and city council declined to pay, which is consistent with FBI guidance. The decision not to pay, however, was not free. Baltimore's total recovery cost exceeded $18 million: IT contractors to rebuild systems, replacement hardware, new software licenses, and the staff time across dozens of departments to reconstruct operations that had to be rebuilt largely from scratch. The $18 million figure does not include the economic cost of the disruption to residents and businesses unable to complete transactions dependent on city systems.
Operational: Weeks without city services for 600,000 residents
Six hundred thousand Baltimore residents experienced weeks of disrupted city services. Water bills could not be paid online — eventually the city waived late fees for the duration of the outage. Property taxes could not be processed electronically. Real estate closings were halted city-wide because title searches require access to property records. Permit applications could not be submitted. City employees across 13 departments conducted operations manually — processing paperwork by hand, using personal phones, and maintaining handwritten records of transactions that would need to be entered into systems once they came back online.
Real estate market: Thousands of transactions halted
The impact on the Baltimore real estate market was one of the most concrete and economically significant effects of the breach. Real estate closings require access to property records to verify title and confirm ownership. With property records inaccessible, real estate attorneys could not perform the title searches required for closing. Thousands of pending property sales were put on hold for weeks. Buyers who had sold other properties or given notice at rental units and were depending on their pending purchases found themselves in difficult positions. The economic damage to individuals caught in this situation extended well beyond the city's own costs.
Lesson: Second major Baltimore ransomware attack in 14 months
The May 2019 attack was not Baltimore's first ransomware incident. In March 2018, just 14 months earlier, Baltimore's 911 dispatch system was hit by ransomware — that attack was contained more quickly but demonstrated that Baltimore's IT infrastructure had known vulnerabilities. The 2019 attack occurred in the same environment that had already been targeted. The failure to implement the controls that would have prevented the 2019 attack — RDP hardening, patch management, segmentation, offline backups — in the 14 months between the two attacks is the most damning aspect of the incident from a governance perspective.
— Lessons Learned
What Baltimore teaches about internet exposure, patching, and recovery
RDP should never be exposed directly to the internet
Remote Desktop Protocol accessible from the public internet is one of the most consistently exploited attack vectors in ransomware operations. The correct control is simple: block RDP at the perimeter firewall and require VPN authentication before any RDP access is permitted. This single architectural change removes the most common ransomware entry point.
Critical patches must be applied on a defined timeline
EternalBlue was patched in March 2017. WannaCry demonstrated its consequences in May 2017. Baltimore had not applied the patch more than two years later. A patch management program with a defined maximum timeline for critical patch application and a tracking system that identifies unpatched systems is the control that prevents known vulnerabilities from remaining exploitable indefinitely.
Network segmentation contains ransomware blast radius
The ransomware spread from its initial entry point to systems across 13 departments because the city had a flat, unsegmented network. Segmentation — placing departments or functions in separate network zones with controlled communication between zones — limits how far ransomware can spread from its initial infection point. Containment does not prevent the attack; it limits the damage.
Offline backups determine recovery cost
Baltimore spent $18 million recovering from a $76,000 ransom demand because it did not have adequate offline backups to restore from. Offline or immutable backups that ransomware cannot reach determine whether recovery is a restoration exercise measured in days or a rebuild exercise measured in months and millions of dollars. Tested offline backups are the control that makes ransomware a recoverable incident rather than an existential one.
