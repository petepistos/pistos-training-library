— What Happened
November 2014 — a studio destroyed by one email
The studio that got wiped
On November 24, 2014, employees at Sony Pictures Entertainment arrived at work to find a skull image on every computer screen in the building. Their machines were locked. A group calling itself the Guardians of Peace had penetrated Sony's network, spent weeks stealing data, then deployed a wiper that destroyed the hard drives of thousands of workstations and servers. Sony's email, phones, and business systems went down simultaneously. Employees could not access anything. The attack was the most destructive cyberattack ever carried out against a US company at that time, and it was triggered by a single phishing email that one employee had opened.
What the attackers took
Before deploying the wiper, the attackers exfiltrated approximately 100 terabytes of data from Sony's network over several weeks. The stolen data included five complete, unreleased films that appeared on piracy sites within days of the attack. It also included the personal information — Social Security numbers, home addresses, salary details — of approximately 47,000 current and former employees. Executives' private email exchanges were published, including messages discussing talent, deals, and internal conflicts. The breadth of what was stolen indicated the attackers had broad, undisturbed access for an extended period before choosing to act.
Who did it and why
The FBI publicly attributed the attack to the Lazarus Group, a North Korean state-sponsored hacking team, within weeks of the incident. The US government concluded the attack was retaliation for The Interview, a Sony comedy film depicting the fictional assassination of North Korean leader Kim Jong-un. North Korea had previously demanded Sony cancel the film's release and had threatened action if it did not. Sony initially canceled the theatrical release following threats against movie theaters, then reversed course under government and public pressure. The US imposed new sanctions on North Korea in direct response to the attack.
The moment employees knew
The attack became visible all at once. On the morning of November 24, the skull image appeared simultaneously on screens across Sony's offices worldwide. The image included a message threatening to release Sony's data if demands were not met. Within hours, the first stolen data appeared on file-sharing sites. Sony's IT staff could not stop the wiper because it had already run. Employees were told to leave their computers off and go home. The company operated without computers, email, or phones for weeks while recovery began.
— How It Happened
From a phishing email to total network destruction
Step 1: Targeted spear-phishing
The Lazarus Group identified Sony executives and employees through publicly available professional information — primarily LinkedIn. They crafted emails tailored to specific recipients, referencing Sony's business context and the recipient's role. At least one email contained a malicious attachment. An employee opened it. The malware executed. The attackers had their initial foothold inside Sony's network.
Step 2: Destover malware deployed
The attackers installed a sophisticated malware toolkit called Destover. It included a backdoor that established persistent command-and-control communication with attacker servers. It also included a credential harvesting module that pulled usernames and passwords from the infected machine — allowing the attackers to authenticate to other Sony systems using legitimate credentials, making their movement much harder to detect through standard monitoring.
Step 3: Months of silent reconnaissance and exfiltration
Having established access in mid-2014, the attackers spent months conducting reconnaissance inside Sony's network. They identified where sensitive data lived, mapped what systems existed, and gradually exfiltrated the 100 terabytes over weeks. Sony had no outbound data loss prevention monitoring capable of detecting sustained, high-volume data theft occurring on its own infrastructure. The theft completed entirely before the destructive phase began.
Step 4: Wiper activated — Destover's final payload
When ready, the attackers activated the wiper component of Destover across Sony's network. The wiper overwrote master boot records and then files, rendering machines unrecoverable. Because Sony's network was flat and unsegmented, the wiper spread from the initial infection to workstations, servers, and backup systems across the enterprise. Thousands of machines were destroyed simultaneously. Physical hard drive replacement was required for every affected machine.
— Root Cause
Four control failures that let a nation-state destroy a studio
Failure 1: Email security did not stop the phish
The entry point was a spear-phishing email with a malicious attachment. Spear-phishing is harder to stop than generic phishing because it uses personalized details that bypass content-based filters. Effective defense requires email gateway filtering, attachment sandboxing that executes files in an isolated environment to detect malicious behavior, and regular user training specifically for targeted phishing. Sony's posture on all three was insufficient to catch a sophisticated, targeted nation-state operation.
Failure 2: Flat network enabled total lateral movement
Once the attackers had a foothold on a single workstation, Sony's flat, unsegmented network gave them access to every other system in the organization. There were no internal zones, no controls requiring re-authentication to move between segments, and no east-west traffic monitoring. A properly segmented network would have contained the initial compromise to a limited blast radius, required the attackers to perform additional exploitation to reach sensitive systems, and made lateral movement visible through traffic anomalies.
Failure 3: No DLP detected 100TB leaving
One hundred terabytes of data left Sony's network over weeks and no alert fired. There was no outbound DLP monitoring and no anomaly detection on traffic volume. Data exfiltration at this scale requires sustained high-bandwidth connections to external destinations. Basic outbound traffic monitoring would have produced alerts. The absence of DLP meant the attackers could complete their entire theft before activating the destructive phase — Sony lost both its data and any chance of early detection.
Failure 4: Backup systems were reachable by the wiper
The wiper destroyed Sony's backup systems alongside its production systems because the backup infrastructure lived on the same flat network. The single control that would have guaranteed recovery capability regardless of how destructive the attack was — an offline or air-gapped backup copy unreachable by any network-delivered payload — did not exist. Recovery required physical replacement of thousands of hard drives and rebuilding systems almost entirely from scratch.
— The Loss
Financial, operational, reputational, and strategic consequences
Financial cost: over $100 million direct
Sony's direct costs exceeded $100 million. Components included approximately $35 million in IT remediation and infrastructure rebuild, legal costs from employee lawsuits over stolen personal data, settlement costs for class action litigation, and crisis communications expenses. Sony's insurance covered a portion. The company booked approximately $15 million net impact in disclosed financials, with the remainder offset by insurance and capitalized as infrastructure investment. The total economic cost, including downstream impacts on film releases and business relationships, was considerably higher.
Operational: Weeks without functioning systems
Sony Pictures operated without email, computers, or phone systems for weeks. Employees communicated by handwriting notes and using personal cell phones. Film production schedules were disrupted. Business negotiations requiring confidential communications could not proceed. Physical hard drives had to be procured and installed in thousands of machines before any digital operations could resume. The recovery effort involved hundreds of outside contractors working across multiple locations simultaneously.
Reputational: Private communications became public record
The publication of Sony executives' private email exchanges produced reputational damage that financial figures cannot capture. Co-chair Amy Pascal's emails — including racially insensitive messages and candid discussions of talent and deals — became front-page news globally and led directly to her resignation. Salary data for thousands of employees was published. The frank, private communications necessary for running any organization were exposed, damaging relationships with talent, studios, and employees simultaneously.
Strategic: A nation-state attacked a private company over a film
The Sony attack established a precedent with no prior example: a nation-state conducting a destructive cyberattack against a private US company over the content of an entertainment product. The FBI's public attribution and the US government's sanctions response confirmed that this category of threat is real and governmentally acknowledged. Every US company with geopolitical exposure — which includes any company operating internationally — must now factor nation-state threat actors into its risk model.
— Lessons Learned
What Sony teaches about phishing, segmentation, and resilience
One phishing email is the most probable entry point
Every sophisticated intrusion begins somewhere accessible. For most organizations, that means email. Spear-phishing works because it is targeted, contextual, and harder to catch with technical filters alone. Email gateway filtering, attachment sandboxing, and regular spear-phishing simulation training are foundational controls that must all function together.
A flat network turns every breach into a total breach
If an attacker who compromises one workstation can reach every server, database, and backup system on the network, then any breach can become a complete destruction event. Segmentation limits blast radius. Without it, the only question after initial compromise is how much the attacker chooses to take or destroy — and the answer depends entirely on their intent.
100TB left the building and nobody noticed
Outbound traffic monitoring and data loss prevention detect exfiltration in progress. High-volume sustained transfers to external destinations are detectable. The absence of these controls at Sony allowed the entire theft to complete before the destructive phase began — meaning Sony lost both its data and any chance of an early-detection response.
Offline backups survive what network-connected backups do not
When a wiper reaches backup systems through a flat network, recovery becomes a physical hardware rebuild. An offline or air-gapped backup is unreachable by any network-delivered malware. Backup resilience is not only a disaster recovery consideration — it is a security control that determines whether recovery from a destructive attack is possible at all.
