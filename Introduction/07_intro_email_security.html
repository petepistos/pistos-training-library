— Email as an Attack Surface
Why email is the most targeted communication channel
Email is the primary delivery channel for cyberattacks
Email is the most widely used business communication tool and, as a direct consequence, the most targeted channel for cyberattacks. The vast majority of malware infections begin with an email. Most phishing attacks arrive by email. Business email compromise fraud — which has cost organizations tens of billions of dollars — is conducted by email. Ransomware is frequently delivered through malicious email attachments. The combination of email's ubiquity, the ease with which it can be spoofed, and the fact that users are conditioned to act on emails quickly makes it uniquely valuable to attackers and uniquely risky for organizations without strong email security practices.
Email authentication standards: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC reduce spoofing
Three email authentication standards work together to make it harder to send email that falsely claims to be from your domain. SPF (Sender Policy Framework) specifies which mail servers are authorized to send email on behalf of your domain. DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) adds a cryptographic signature to outgoing messages that receiving servers can verify. DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) tells receiving servers what to do when messages fail SPF or DKIM checks — quarantine them or reject them outright. When all three are correctly configured, it becomes significantly harder for attackers to send email that appears to come from your organization's domain to recipients outside your organization.
Email encryption protects message content in transit and at rest
Standard email transmission does not guarantee that message content is encrypted — email can be intercepted and read as it travels between servers. Transport Layer Security encrypts email in transit between mail servers, and most major email providers implement this. End-to-end encryption — using protocols like S/MIME or PGP — encrypts message content so that only the intended recipient can decrypt it, even if the message is intercepted. For most business email, TLS in transit is sufficient. For highly sensitive communications — legal, financial, healthcare — end-to-end encryption provides stronger protection. Data loss prevention tools can also prevent sensitive content from being emailed to unauthorized recipients.
Personal email on business devices creates risk
Using personal email accounts on business devices — or using business email for personal purposes — creates security and compliance problems. Personal email services may have weaker security controls than organizational email. Forwarding business email to personal accounts bypasses organizational data loss prevention and retention controls. Personal email accessed on a business device can be a source of malware that compromises the device and any business resources it can access. Many organizations have policies that prohibit personal email use on business devices or prohibit forwarding business email externally. These policies exist because the mixing of personal and business communication channels consistently creates exposure.
— Identifying Malicious Email
The signs that distinguish legitimate email from attacks
Check the sender's actual email address, not just the display name
The most common technique in phishing emails is display name spoofing — showing a trusted name in the sender field while the actual sending address is completely different. An email might display 'Microsoft Security Team' or your CEO's name while the actual sending address is 'security@random-domain.xyz'. Most email clients show only the display name until you click to expand the sender details. Checking the actual email address — not just the name displayed — is the single most effective habit for catching phishing. In business email compromise attacks, the sender address may be a domain that looks like your organization's domain with subtle differences: 'company-inc.com' instead of 'company.com', or 'company.co' instead of 'company.com'.
Inspect links before clicking: the displayed URL may differ from the actual destination
Phishing emails frequently display a legitimate-looking URL as the visible text of a link while the actual hyperlink destination is a malicious domain. In a desktop email client, hovering the mouse over a link without clicking it typically reveals the actual destination URL in the status bar or a tooltip. On mobile devices, pressing and holding a link without releasing usually shows the actual destination. The actual destination domain is what matters — not the text displayed, not the logo next to the link, not the formatting around it. If the actual destination domain does not match the legitimate service you would expect to be taken to, do not click. When in doubt, navigate directly to the service by typing the known URL in the browser.
Unexpected attachments with urgency are a consistent malware delivery pattern
Malicious email attachments often arrive in messages that create urgency — an overdue invoice, a time-sensitive contract, a security alert requiring immediate action. The urgency is designed to prompt the recipient to open the attachment before thinking carefully about whether it makes sense. Common attachment types used to deliver malware include Office documents with macros (Word, Excel), PDFs with embedded scripts, and compressed archives (ZIP, RAR) containing executables. Legitimate business communications do not typically require enabling macros or disabling security warnings to view content. Any message that creates urgency around opening an attachment and requires bypassing security prompts should be treated as highly suspicious.
When in doubt, verify through a separate channel before taking any action
Uncertainty about whether an email is legitimate is a signal to verify, not to guess. If you receive an email from a colleague asking you to take an unusual action — wire a payment, share credentials, click a link — and something about it seems off, contact the apparent sender through a completely separate channel: call them on a known number, message them through a different platform, or walk to their desk. Do not reply to the suspicious email to ask if it is real — if the email is a compromise of your colleague's account, the attacker controls the reply. Verification through a separate channel takes minutes and breaks the attacker's control of the communication.
— Safe Email Practices
Habits that reduce email-based risk
Do not open attachments from unexpected sources without verification
Attachments from senders you do not recognize or from senders you know but in an unexpected context deserve careful scrutiny before opening. A PDF from an unfamiliar vendor, a Word document from a contact who has never sent you a document before, or a ZIP file from an internal address at an unusual time are all worth pausing on. When in doubt, contact the sender through a separate channel to confirm they sent the attachment and describe what it is. IT or security teams can also scan attachments before they are opened if there is uncertainty. The cost of pausing to verify is seconds to minutes; the cost of opening a malicious attachment can be days of incident response.
Be cautious with email that asks you to log in through a link
A very common phishing technique involves sending an email that claims to be from a service the recipient uses — Microsoft, Google, a bank, a cloud application — and directing them to click a link to log in and resolve an issue. The link leads to a credential-harvesting page that looks exactly like the legitimate service. The defense is simple: never log in to any service by clicking a link in an email. Instead, open the service by navigating directly to its address in the browser, or use a bookmark to a URL you have previously verified. If the service genuinely requires your attention, you will see the alert or notice when you log in directly. This one habit eliminates credential harvesting through phishing links.
Protect sensitive information sent by email
Email is not inherently a secure channel for sensitive information. Emails can be misdirected to wrong recipients, intercepted in transit, forwarded beyond the intended audience, and retained indefinitely in archives. Before sending sensitive information by email — financial data, personal records, legal documents, credentials — consider whether email is the appropriate channel, whether encryption is needed, and whether data loss prevention policies apply. Organizations typically have policies about what types of information can be sent by email and to whom. When sensitive information must be sent externally, secure file sharing platforms or encrypted email may be required. Sending a credential, a social security number, or a financial account number in a plain email creates exposure that persists in email archives.
Report phishing to your security team — it protects everyone
When you identify a phishing email, the most valuable action you can take is to report it to your security or IT team using the designated reporting mechanism — typically a 'Report Phishing' button in the email client or a specific email address. Simply deleting the email provides no benefit to anyone else in the organization who may have received the same message. A reported phishing campaign allows IT to block the sending domain, alert other employees, and determine whether anyone has already responded and may need immediate assistance. Even if you recognized the phishing attempt easily, reporting it contributes to the collective defense. Organizations that have high phishing report rates tend to have better detection and faster response when actual incidents occur.
— Email and Compliance
Regulatory requirements and organizational policies around email
Email retention policies govern how long messages must be kept
Many regulatory frameworks require organizations to retain business communications — including email — for defined periods. Financial services regulations, healthcare regulations, and legal requirements may mandate that email records be preserved and producible for audits, investigations, or litigation. Organizations typically implement retention policies through their email platform, which automatically archives messages for the required period. Individual users should understand that business email on organizational systems is subject to retention requirements, is potentially discoverable in legal proceedings, and should be composed with the same care as any formal business communication. Deleting business email to avoid retention is typically a policy and potentially a legal violation.
Data loss prevention tools monitor email for sensitive content
Data loss prevention (DLP) systems monitor outbound email — and sometimes inbound email — for content that matches patterns associated with sensitive information: credit card numbers, social security numbers, medical record numbers, specific document classifications. When a DLP policy is triggered, the message may be blocked, flagged for review, or delivered with a compliance notice. DLP is not surveillance of personal communications — it is a technical control designed to prevent accidental or intentional exfiltration of sensitive data through email. Understanding that DLP is in place helps users avoid accidental policy violations, such as emailing a customer list to a personal account for convenience.
External email warnings protect against spoofed internal addresses
Many organizations add a banner or warning to email messages that originate from outside the organization — even when those messages display an internal employee's name or email address. This banner, typically shown in yellow or orange, alerts the recipient that the message did not originate from within the organization's email system. This warning is a defense against business email compromise and spoofing attacks. An email displaying a colleague's name and internal email address, combined with an external email warning, is a strong signal that the message is a spoofing or BEC attempt. Learning to look for and understand this warning is a useful habit for identifying attacks that impersonate internal personnel.
Account security for email: MFA and strong passwords are particularly critical
Business email accounts deserve especially strong security because email is used for password resets across virtually every other service. A compromised email account gives an attacker access to the password reset mechanism for every service associated with that email address. This makes email account security a priority within credential security. MFA on business email is non-negotiable — it should be the first place MFA is enabled. Strong, unique passwords for email accounts follow the same logic. Reviewing email account security settings periodically — checking for unauthorized forwarding rules, unexpected connected applications, or unusual login activity in the account security log — is a good maintenance practice.
