— What Is Quantum Computing
How it differs from the computers we use today
Classical computers process information as binary bits: 0 or 1
Every computer in use today — laptops, servers, smartphones, cloud infrastructure — processes information using bits. A bit is the smallest unit of data and can represent exactly one of two states: 0 or 1. All computation, regardless of complexity, is built from operations on these binary values. The processors in modern computers can perform billions of these operations per second, making them extraordinarily capable for most tasks. However, for certain types of mathematical problems — particularly those involving searching through enormous numbers of possible solutions — the binary approach imposes fundamental limits on how fast the calculation can be completed.
Quantum computers use qubits that can represent 0, 1, or both simultaneously
Quantum computers use units called qubits, which exploit quantum mechanical properties to behave differently from classical bits. While a classical bit must be either 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a state called superposition — representing 0 and 1 at the same time until it is measured. A quantum computer with multiple qubits in superposition can evaluate many possible solutions simultaneously rather than sequentially. For the specific types of mathematical problems that underpin modern encryption, this capability represents a potential computational shortcut of enormous magnitude — the difference between a task taking millions of years and a task taking hours.
Quantum computing is specialized, not a general-purpose replacement for classical computers
Quantum computers are not better at everything. They are specifically powerful for a narrow category of mathematical problems: prime number factoring, discrete logarithm calculation, and searching unsorted datasets. For most computing tasks — browsing the web, running business applications, processing transactions — quantum computers offer no meaningful advantage and are far more difficult to build and maintain than classical systems. The cybersecurity relevance of quantum computing is almost entirely in that narrow category of problems, because those specific problems are the mathematical foundation of most encryption systems in use today.
Practical quantum computers at scale remain a future challenge, but the timeline is shortening
Building a quantum computer capable of breaking modern encryption requires not just qubits but high-quality, error-corrected qubits operating at scale — numbers ranging from hundreds of thousands to millions of logical qubits, depending on the algorithm and key size. Current quantum hardware operates with hundreds or a few thousand physical qubits with significant error rates. The gap between current capability and the threshold needed to threaten encryption is real, but researchers and national intelligence agencies treat it as a matter of when, not if. Preparation for quantum-resistant cryptography is already underway across governments and large enterprises.
— How Quantum Threatens Encryption
Why the math that secures the internet is at risk
Modern encryption relies on mathematical problems that are easy in one direction and hard in the other
The encryption systems that protect internet communications, financial transactions, and stored data are built on mathematical relationships that are computationally asymmetric. Multiplying two large prime numbers is easy — even for classical computers. But given only the product, finding the two original primes is extraordinarily difficult. This asymmetry underpins RSA encryption, the algorithm protecting much of today's web traffic. The difficulty of factoring large numbers is what makes the private key effectively unguessable, even when the public key is openly shared. The entire security model depends on that factoring problem remaining computationally hard.
Shor's algorithm allows a quantum computer to factor large numbers exponentially faster
In 1994, mathematician Peter Shor demonstrated that a quantum computer could factor large numbers exponentially faster than any known classical algorithm — reducing a calculation that would take classical computers millions of years to one that could be completed in hours or days on a sufficiently capable quantum machine. This directly threatens RSA and similar public-key encryption schemes. A quantum computer running Shor's algorithm with sufficient qubit quality and quantity could derive the private key from the public key, breaking the encryption protecting that communication or stored data. The algorithm is proven; only the hardware required to run it at scale is the remaining barrier.
Grover's algorithm weakens symmetric encryption by effectively halving the key length
A second quantum algorithm, Grover's algorithm, applies to symmetric encryption systems — the type used to protect stored data and secure connections after the initial key exchange. Rather than breaking symmetric encryption outright, Grover's algorithm speeds up the brute-force search for a key by providing a quadratic speedup. In practical terms, this effectively halves the security of a symmetric key: a 128-bit key would provide only 64 bits of effective security against a quantum attacker. The mitigation is straightforward — use 256-bit symmetric keys instead of 128-bit keys — which is why this threat, while real, is considered more manageable than the threat to public-key cryptography.
Harvest now, decrypt later: the threat exists even before quantum computers are built
A particularly serious risk is the harvest now, decrypt later attack. Adversaries — state actors in particular — are recording encrypted communications and data today, with the intent to decrypt them once capable quantum computers become available. This means that data encrypted using today's vulnerable algorithms is already at risk of future exposure, even though the quantum computers needed to decrypt it do not yet exist. Sensitive information that must remain confidential for ten, twenty, or thirty years is at risk today, not in some distant future. This is why migration to quantum-resistant cryptography is a current priority for governments and regulated industries.
— Post-Quantum Cryptography
The new encryption standards designed to resist quantum attacks
Post-quantum cryptography uses mathematical problems that quantum computers cannot solve efficiently
Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) refers to encryption algorithms based on mathematical problems that are believed to be resistant to quantum computer attacks — not just classical computers. These problems are drawn from areas of mathematics where no known efficient quantum algorithm exists: lattice problems, hash-based structures, error-correcting codes, and multivariate equations. The goal is to replace or supplement current encryption algorithms with options that remain secure against both classical and quantum adversaries. Importantly, post-quantum algorithms are designed to run on classical computers — the transition does not require any quantum hardware.
NIST has standardized the first post-quantum cryptographic algorithms
The U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology ran a multi-year competition to evaluate and standardize post-quantum cryptographic algorithms. In 2024, NIST published the first finalized post-quantum standards: CRYSTALS-Kyber for key encapsulation and CRYSTALS-Dilithium for digital signatures. These algorithms are based on the hardness of lattice problems, which are believed to be resistant to both classical and quantum attacks. Technology vendors and standards bodies are now incorporating these algorithms into protocols, and migration guidance has been issued for federal systems and will progressively reach commercial sectors.
Migration to post-quantum cryptography is a complex, multi-year organizational effort
Transitioning an organization's cryptographic infrastructure to post-quantum algorithms is not a simple software update. It requires identifying all places where cryptography is in use — encrypted communications, digital certificates, stored data, API authentication, VPN tunnels — and developing a plan for migrating each to quantum-resistant alternatives. Many of these systems are embedded in hardware, vendor products, and long-lived certificates that cannot be updated overnight. Organizations that begin this inventory and planning now will be better positioned to meet regulatory requirements and protect sensitive data as quantum threats mature.
Crypto agility: the ability to change encryption algorithms quickly is a strategic capability
One of the lessons of the quantum threat is the importance of crypto agility — building systems in a way that allows the encryption algorithms they use to be changed without requiring a complete system rebuild. Organizations that hard-coded specific algorithms into infrastructure, applications, and hardware are in the most difficult position when those algorithms need to be replaced. Designing for crypto agility — using algorithm-independent interfaces, keeping encryption components modular, and maintaining a cryptographic inventory — is a forward-looking security practice that applies to quantum migration and any future cryptographic evolution.
— What This Means for Your Organization
Practical awareness and the steps being taken now
The quantum threat is real and the preparation timeline is shorter than it appears
Cryptographically relevant quantum computers — capable of breaking the encryption protecting today's sensitive data — may be years or decades away. But the harvest now, decrypt later threat means that data being encrypted today with vulnerable algorithms is already at risk of future exposure. For data that must remain confidential for a decade or more — medical records, financial data, intellectual property, government communications — the exposure window may already be open. Regulatory and compliance frameworks are beginning to require quantum-readiness planning, and organizations that treat this as a distant future problem may find themselves unprepared when requirements arrive.
Awareness of certificate and key lifespans is an important starting point
Every digital certificate and cryptographic key has an algorithm, a key size, and an expiration date. Certificates using RSA with key sizes below 2048 bits are already considered weak by current standards — quantum threats aside. Certificates with long validity periods issued today using currently vulnerable algorithms may still be active when quantum threats materialize. Knowing what cryptography your organization uses, where it is used, and when certificates expire is the first step toward a transition plan. Security and IT teams are typically responsible for this inventory, but awareness of the issue across the organization supports better decision-making.
Vendor and supply chain cryptographic dependencies create additional complexity
Most organizations rely on software, hardware, and services from vendors whose cryptographic implementations they do not directly control. A cloud service provider, a payment processor, a network appliance vendor — each uses encryption that will need to be updated as post-quantum standards are adopted. Organizations should ask their vendors about their post-quantum migration plans and timelines, include cryptographic currency in vendor risk assessments, and prefer vendors who demonstrate awareness of and planning for quantum-related cryptographic migration. Supply chain cryptographic risk is an area where awareness translates directly into better procurement and risk management decisions.
Quantum computing also enables new capabilities that benefit security and other fields
Quantum computing is not only a threat to be managed — it also enables new capabilities. Quantum key distribution (QKD) uses quantum mechanical properties to distribute cryptographic keys in a way that is theoretically impossible to intercept without detection. Quantum computing also offers significant potential in drug discovery, materials science, financial optimization, and machine learning. For cybersecurity specifically, quantum-enhanced random number generation and quantum-accelerated pattern recognition are being researched as tools for improving defenses. The technology is dual-use, and organizations that understand both its threats and its opportunities are better positioned to navigate its development.
— Summary
What you have learned in this module
Quantum computers use qubits in superposition to evaluate many solutions simultaneously
Classical computers process bits as 0 or 1 sequentially. Quantum computers use qubits that can be in superposition, enabling parallel evaluation of solutions for specific mathematical problems. This capability threatens the mathematical foundations of most current encryption.
Shor's algorithm breaks public-key encryption; Grover's algorithm weakens symmetric encryption
Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers exponentially faster, threatening RSA and similar systems. Grover's algorithm halves the effective length of symmetric keys. The mitigation for Grover's threat is using 256-bit keys; Shor's threat requires new algorithms.
Post-quantum cryptographic algorithms standardized by NIST are designed to resist both threats
NIST has published post-quantum standards based on lattice problems. These run on classical computers and are being incorporated into protocols and systems. Transitioning requires a cryptographic inventory and multi-year migration planning.
Harvest now, decrypt later means the threat to long-lived sensitive data is present today
State actors are collecting encrypted data today to decrypt when quantum computers become available. Data that must remain confidential for a decade or more may already be at risk. Quantum-readiness planning is a current organizational priority, not a future one.
