— Why Passwords Matter
The primary key to every system you use
Passwords are the most widely used authentication mechanism — and the most frequently compromised
Every system you access — email, cloud applications, financial platforms, internal tools — requires a password to verify your identity. Passwords are the most prevalent authentication control in use today. They are also one of the most consistently exploited weaknesses in organizational security. Billions of username and password combinations from data breaches over the past decade are available on criminal marketplaces. Attackers use automated tools that test these combinations against services where users may have reused the same password, and separate tools that test millions of common password variations per second. The prevalence of password-based authentication makes password hygiene a foundational individual security responsibility.
Credential stuffing: reused passwords turn one breach into many
Credential stuffing is the automated use of stolen username and password combinations — obtained from one breached service — to attempt login at other services. It is effective precisely because people reuse passwords. If your password for a retail website is the same as your business email password, a breach of the retail site gives an attacker a valid credential for your email account. Because email accounts are used for password resets on most other services, one compromised email account becomes a key that unlocks many others. The defense is simple and absolute: every account must have a unique password. A password manager makes this achievable without memorizing dozens of different credentials.
Password spraying: attackers test the most common passwords against many accounts
Password spraying is an attack technique in which an attacker tries a small number of very common passwords — 'Password1', 'Welcome1', 'Summer2024' — against a large number of accounts. Unlike brute force attacks that try many passwords against one account, spraying avoids account lockout thresholds by trying few passwords per account. It is particularly effective against organizations that have not enforced strong password policies, because predictable password patterns are extremely common: people tend to use a word or name followed by a number and possibly an exclamation point. Enforcing minimum length, complexity requirements, and screening new passwords against lists of known common passwords reduces vulnerability to spraying attacks.
Phishing harvests passwords directly from users who enter them into fake login pages
A significant portion of credential compromise occurs not through technical attacks but through deception. Phishing emails direct users to convincing fake login pages — pages that look exactly like Microsoft, Google, or a business application login screen — where the user enters their credentials believing they are logging in legitimately. Those credentials are immediately captured by the attacker. Unlike brute force or credential stuffing, phishing works regardless of password strength or uniqueness — even a strong, unique password is compromised if the user is deceived into entering it on an attacker-controlled page. This is why MFA is essential: it provides protection even when credentials are phished.
— Strong Password Practices
What makes a password strong and how to manage many of them
Length is the most important factor in password strength
The most important characteristic of a strong password is length. A 16-character password is exponentially harder to crack through brute force than an 8-character password, even if the shorter one includes symbols and numbers. Every additional character multiplies the number of possible combinations an attacker must test. Modern security guidance has shifted away from requiring complex character combinations — which produce short, barely-changed passwords like 'P@ssw0rd' — toward requiring length, with passphrases of 16 or more characters being more resistant to cracking and easier to remember. A phrase like 'coffee-mountain-river-lamp' is long, memorable, and significantly stronger than a typical complex password requirement produces.
Uniqueness: every account must have a different password
Using the same password across multiple accounts is one of the most significant and most common security mistakes. When a service you use is breached — and services are breached regularly — the attacker now has a credential that works on every other service where you used the same password. Password uniqueness is non-negotiable as a security practice. The practical objection — that it is impossible to remember dozens of different complex passwords — is valid and is the reason password managers exist. A password manager generates, stores, and automatically fills unique, strong passwords for every account, requiring you to remember only one strong master password.
Password managers: the practical solution to unique strong passwords for every account
A password manager is an application that generates and securely stores unique, strong passwords for every account you use. You create one strong master password to access the manager; the manager handles everything else. When you navigate to a login page, the manager fills your credentials automatically. Because the manager generates and stores the passwords, you never need to know or remember them individually — and because they are generated rather than chosen, they are not susceptible to dictionary attacks or guessing. Password managers are available for personal use as standalone applications and are often deployed organizationally by IT departments. Using a password manager is one of the highest-impact individual security practices available.
What not to do: the password mistakes that create the most risk
Common password mistakes create predictable patterns that attackers specifically look for. Using the current year or season in a password — 'Summer2024' — is predictable enough that spraying tools include these variations. Using personal information — names, birthdays, pet names, sports teams — is discoverable through social media research. Using keyboard patterns like 'qwerty' or '123456' appears in every attacker's first test. Reusing passwords with minor variations — changing 'Password1' to 'Password2' when required to update — is anticipated by cracking tools that test common mutations. Storing passwords in unencrypted files, sticky notes, or shared documents exposes them to anyone with access to those resources.
— MFA and Access Controls
Adding layers beyond the password
Multifactor authentication provides protection even when passwords are compromised
Multifactor authentication requires a second verification factor beyond the password — something you have (a phone or hardware key) or something you are (a fingerprint). The critical property of MFA is that it protects accounts even when the password has been stolen. If an attacker obtains your password through phishing, a data breach, or credential stuffing, they still cannot log in without your second factor. This makes MFA one of the most important security controls available. Research consistently shows that accounts protected by MFA are dramatically less likely to be compromised. Enabling MFA on every account — starting with business email, cloud platforms, and financial systems — is a high-priority action.
Account lockout and login monitoring detect and limit brute force attacks
Technical controls on the authentication system itself limit the effectiveness of brute force and credential stuffing attacks. Account lockout policies temporarily lock accounts after a defined number of failed login attempts, preventing automated tools from testing thousands of password combinations per second. Login monitoring and anomaly detection identify suspicious patterns: login attempts from unfamiliar countries, login attempts at unusual hours, or large numbers of failed attempts across many accounts. These controls are typically implemented at the system or application level and are the responsibility of IT and security teams, but users benefit from understanding that these controls exist and reporting unusual login notifications.
Privileged accounts require stronger controls than standard user accounts
Accounts with elevated permissions — administrator accounts, accounts that can access sensitive databases, accounts that can modify security configurations — represent a higher risk if compromised and therefore warrant stronger authentication controls. Privileged accounts should use the strongest available MFA (hardware keys rather than SMS), should be subject to more restrictive policies, and in many cases should be separate accounts used only for administrative tasks rather than daily user activities. An administrator who uses the same account for browsing email and managing servers creates a situation where a phishing attack that compromises the email activity simultaneously compromises the administrative capability.
Password policies: what organizations require and why
Organizational password policies define minimum standards for the passwords used to access business systems. Common requirements include minimum length (typically 12-16 characters), prohibition of previously used passwords, prohibition of passwords that appear on known-compromised password lists, and mandatory MFA for all accounts. Some organizations require regular password changes; current guidance from NIST and other authorities recommends against forced regular changes unless there is evidence of compromise, as they tend to produce predictable password patterns ('Password1' → 'Password2'). Understanding why policies exist — to prevent specific attack types — helps people comply with the intent of the policy rather than looking for minimum-effort workarounds.
— Protecting Your Credentials
Individual behaviors that keep accounts secure
Never share your password — not with IT, not with colleagues, not with anyone
A legitimate IT department will never ask for your password. If someone contacts you claiming to be IT support and asks for your credentials, it is either a social engineering attack or a process violation — in either case, the correct response is to decline. Sharing passwords with colleagues — even for legitimate convenience purposes like account sharing or backup access — creates audit gaps, transfers your credential to someone who may have weaker security practices, and in the event of an incident makes it impossible to determine who took an action. Organizations that need shared access to resources should use shared accounts with appropriate controls, not personal credential sharing.
Recognize phishing: check where you are before entering credentials
Before entering a username and password on any login page, verify that you are actually on the legitimate service's website. Check the URL in the browser address bar — not just the text displayed on the page, but the actual domain in the address bar. A phishing page for Microsoft might display Microsoft logos and text but have a domain like 'microsoft-secure-login.com' rather than 'microsoft.com'. Browser password managers provide a useful secondary check: if the manager does not offer to fill your credentials on a page, it may not recognize the domain as the legitimate service you use. When in doubt, navigate directly to the service by typing the known URL rather than following a link in an email.
Handle password reset requests carefully — attackers use account recovery as an attack vector
Password reset mechanisms are a frequent target for attackers because they can be used to take over accounts without knowing the current password. If you receive an unsolicited password reset email for an account — a reset you did not initiate — treat it as a security event: someone may be attempting to access your account. Do not click the reset link. Instead, log in to the service directly through your usual method and verify that your account settings have not been changed. Report the unsolicited reset to your IT or security team. If you do need to reset a legitimate password, navigate directly to the service rather than following a link in an email.
Treat credentials as sensitive information — store and transmit them accordingly
Passwords and credentials are sensitive information that deserve the same protection as financial data or personal records. They should never be written on physical notes kept near a workstation, stored in unprotected documents on shared drives, sent by email or chat, or spoken aloud in environments where they could be overheard. Password managers handle storage securely. If you must share a credential temporarily for a legitimate business reason, use a method that does not leave a permanent record — a secure password sharing feature in an enterprise password manager, for example — and change the password afterward. Treating credentials as sensitive information by default prevents casual handling that creates unnecessary exposure.
— Summary
Key concepts from this module
Credential attacks — stuffing, spraying, and phishing — exploit password reuse and weak patterns
Each attack type has a specific defense: unique passwords stop stuffing; strong long passwords and good policies stop spraying; MFA stops phishing. No single defense works against all three — they are needed together.
Length is the most important password strength factor; uniqueness is non-negotiable
A 16+ character password is substantially stronger than a complex short one. Every account must have a different password. A password manager makes both requirements achievable without memorization.
MFA protects accounts even when passwords are compromised
Enabling MFA on all accounts — especially email, cloud platforms, and financial systems — is the highest-impact authentication control. Even a phished password cannot be used without the second factor.
Never share passwords; verify login pages before entering credentials; report suspicious reset emails
Credential protection is both a technical and behavioral practice. Password managers, MFA, and strong policies provide the technical foundation. Careful individual behavior — verification, non-sharing, reporting — makes the controls effective.
