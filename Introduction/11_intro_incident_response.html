— What Is a Security Incident
Types of incidents users are positioned to discover
A security incident is any event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of systems or data
A security incident is any event that has or may have compromised the security of organizational systems or data. Incidents range in severity from minor — a single phishing email that was caught and not acted on — to critical — a ransomware attack encrypting the organization's file servers. Common incident types include: unauthorized access to systems or data; malware infection on one or more devices; phishing attacks, whether successful or not; data loss or exposure, including accidental misdelivery of sensitive information; account compromise; denial of service attacks; and physical security incidents involving organizational devices. Not every suspicious event becomes an incident, but every suspicious event warrants investigation to determine whether it is one.
Security incidents are frequently discovered by users, not automated systems
A common misconception is that security incidents are always detected by automated tools — antivirus alerts, intrusion detection systems, or security monitoring dashboards. In practice, a significant proportion of security incidents are first noticed by employees going about their regular work: a user notices that files they did not encrypt are now inaccessible, a manager receives a call from a customer asking about a phishing email purportedly from the organization, a finance employee receives a suspicious wire transfer request, or an IT administrator notices unusual login activity in audit logs. Users who recognize these signals and report them promptly enable faster response — which directly limits the damage an incident causes.
What does not constitute an incident — and what does
Understanding the boundary between normal operational issues and security incidents helps in making the right reporting decision. A computer that runs slowly, a forgotten password, a software application that crashes — these are technical issues to report to the helpdesk, not security incidents. A computer that runs slowly after an unusual email attachment was opened, a password that was shared with someone else and may have been used, or a software application that was installed from an unofficial source — these are security concerns warranting investigation. The key question is whether the issue might involve unauthorized access, data exposure, or a compromise of security controls. When in doubt, report to the security team — they can determine whether investigation is warranted.
Early reporting is the most important individual contribution to incident response
The damage caused by a security incident is directly related to how long it goes undetected. An attacker who has been in an organization's systems for thirty minutes and has been detected has caused less damage than one who has been present for thirty days undetected. Ransomware that is detected within minutes of execution, before it has encrypted more than a fraction of the target files, is significantly more recoverable than ransomware discovered after it has encrypted everything and deleted the backups. Every hour that an incident continues before detection represents additional data accessed, additional files encrypted, additional attacker footholds established. The employee who notices something unusual and reports it immediately — rather than waiting to see if it gets worse or trying to resolve it themselves — is making the most valuable possible contribution to incident response.
— Recognizing Incidents
The signals that indicate a potential security incident
Unexpected system behavior that could indicate malware or unauthorized access
Certain system behaviors are consistent with malware infection or unauthorized access and should be treated as security signals rather than routine technical problems. Files that cannot be opened and show unfamiliar extensions suggest ransomware encryption. Processes consuming unusual CPU or network resources may indicate malware activity. Security software that has been disabled or appears to have been tampered with warrants investigation. Browser behavior changes — new toolbars, unexpected redirections, homepage changes — may indicate unwanted software installation. Accounts that show login activity that the account owner did not perform, or that display settings changes the owner did not make, indicate account compromise. None of these observations are definitive by themselves, but each warrants reporting rather than ignoring.
Suspicious email activity — unexpected resets, login attempts, or messages sent without your knowledge
Email accounts are frequently targeted for compromise, and several specific signals indicate possible account compromise. Receiving password reset emails you did not request may mean someone is attempting to take over the account. Receiving notifications of login attempts from unfamiliar locations or devices suggests the credentials have been compromised. Finding email in your sent folder that you did not send, or finding email in your deleted folder that you did not delete, indicates that someone else has been operating your email account. Contacts reporting that they received strange messages from your email address is a strong signal of account compromise. Any of these observations should be reported immediately and the account password changed while awaiting security team guidance.
Data anomalies — missing files, unexpected changes, or unauthorized access notifications
Data-related incidents often produce specific observable signals. Files that were present are now missing or inaccessible. Files that should not have changed have been modified. Audit logs — if the user has access to them — show access by accounts that should not have been accessing that data. Cloud storage services send notifications of new logins or unusual activity. A colleague or customer reports receiving data that should not have been shared with them. These signals may indicate deliberate data theft, ransomware activity, accidental misconfiguration, or an account compromise that resulted in unauthorized data access. Each warrants prompt reporting to the security team for investigation.
Physical security signals — lost devices, unauthorized access, unfamiliar hardware
Physical security incidents can have significant digital security consequences. A lost or stolen laptop may expose the data stored on it, enable access to accounts with saved credentials, and potentially allow someone to access organizational systems. An unfamiliar device — a USB drive, a small device attached to a network port or a keyboard — may be a hardware-based attack tool. An unfamiliar person observed in a secure area of the workplace, or evidence that a physical area has been accessed when it should not have been, warrants reporting to both physical security and the IT team. Physical and digital security are not separate domains — physical access frequently enables digital access.
— How to Report an Incident
The right way to respond when you suspect a security incident
Know your reporting channels before you need them
The most common reason employees delay reporting suspected security incidents is uncertainty about who to contact and how. This uncertainty is best resolved before an incident occurs. Most organizations have a designated reporting mechanism: a specific email address for security incidents, a helpdesk ticketing category, a phone number for urgent security issues, or a direct contact for the security team. Some organizations have a dedicated security operations center with a 24-hour contact for critical incidents. Take a moment now to learn the reporting channel your organization uses. If you are not sure, ask your manager or IT department. Having this information available — ideally saved in a place accessible even if your work computer is unavailable — means you can report immediately when needed.
What to include when reporting: be specific about what you observed and when
When reporting a suspected incident, provide as much specific information as you can: what you observed (specific error messages, file names, account behavior), when you first noticed it, what actions you had taken immediately before noticing it (what file you opened, what link you clicked, what device you connected), which systems are affected, and what you have done since noticing it. The security team uses this information to assess scope and begin investigation. Precise information speeds the response. 'My computer has been acting weird' is less actionable than 'I opened an email attachment from an unfamiliar sender about two hours ago and since then my files have unfamiliar extensions and I cannot open them.' Do not over-analyze whether the incident is real before reporting — report what you observed and let the security team assess.
What to do (and not do) while waiting for the security team to respond
When you suspect an active security incident, there are actions that help and actions that can make the situation worse. Do: stop using the affected system if possible, note everything you observed (write it down), disconnect the device from the network if instructed to by IT (or if you observe active data loss and cannot reach IT immediately), and make yourself available to the security team for questions. Do not: attempt to clean malware yourself (this may destroy forensic evidence), delete suspicious emails before the security team can examine them, turn off the device (which may destroy evidence in memory), or discuss the incident on social media or with parties outside the organization. The security team's ability to investigate depends on preserving evidence and containing the incident — individual actions that seem helpful can inadvertently undermine both.
After the incident: contributing to lessons learned
Security incidents, regardless of how they are resolved, generate information that improves an organization's security posture if properly analyzed. After an incident is resolved, most organizations conduct a post-incident review: identifying how the attacker gained access, what controls failed or were absent, what response actions were effective, and what should be done differently. Employees who participated in the incident — whether as the person who first noticed something or as someone affected by the impact — can contribute valuable perspective to this review. Being open about what happened, what decisions were made, and what information was available at the time enables better analysis. The goal is not blame but improvement. Organizations that treat incidents as learning opportunities develop stronger security over time than those that treat them as failures to be minimized.
— The Incident Response Process
What happens after an incident is reported
Detection and analysis: confirming the incident and assessing scope
When an incident is reported, the security team's first task is to confirm that a security incident has occurred and to assess its scope. This involves examining the affected systems, reviewing logs, correlating events across multiple data sources, and determining what has been accessed, modified, or affected. The detection and analysis phase may reveal that what was reported as a potential incident is a technical issue without security implications — or it may reveal that the reported event is the visible tip of a much larger compromise that has been in progress for some time. Rapid and accurate assessment of scope is essential for making appropriate containment decisions.
Containment: stopping the spread and limiting further damage
Once an incident is confirmed, the security team takes action to contain it — preventing the attacker from accessing additional systems or data, stopping ongoing malware activity, and limiting the damage that continues to accumulate. Containment actions may include: isolating affected systems from the network to prevent spread, forcing password resets for compromised accounts, blocking malicious IP addresses and domains at the firewall, revoking compromised credentials, and disabling specific services or access. The goal is to stop the incident from getting worse while preserving as much forensic evidence as possible. Containment decisions involve tradeoffs — more aggressive containment may cause more operational disruption but stops damage faster.
Eradication and recovery: removing the attacker and restoring operations
After containment, the security team works to eradicate the attacker's presence from the environment: removing malware from affected systems, closing the vulnerability or access path used for initial entry, changing all potentially compromised credentials, and verifying that no attacker persistence mechanisms remain. Only after thorough eradication is recovery safe to begin. Recovery involves restoring affected systems from clean backups, verifying the integrity of restored systems, and gradually returning systems to production in a monitored state. Rushing recovery before eradication is complete risks re-infection from attacker persistence that was not identified. The sequence — contain, eradicate, then recover — exists specifically to avoid this failure mode.
Notification and regulatory requirements: who must be told about an incident
Many security incidents involving personal data or regulated information trigger mandatory notification requirements. Regulatory frameworks governing personal data typically require notification to affected individuals and regulators within specific timeframes — sometimes as short as 72 hours from discovery. Industry-specific regulations may have additional requirements. Insurance carriers may have notification requirements as a condition of coverage. Business partners and vendors may have contractual notification obligations. The security and legal teams determine what notification is required based on the nature of the incident and the data involved. Employees who discover an incident and report it promptly contribute to the organization's ability to meet these obligations — delayed discovery means compressed notification timelines.
— Summary
Key concepts from this module
A security incident is any event threatening confidentiality, integrity, or availability — and many are first spotted by users
Employees are often the first to notice incidents — unusual file behavior, unexpected account activity, suspicious requests. Early reporting is the most valuable individual contribution to incident response.
Know the warning signs: unexpected system behavior, email anomalies, data changes, physical security events
Each category of incident produces observable signals. Recognizing these patterns — and reporting rather than ignoring or attempting to resolve them personally — enables faster organizational response.
Know your reporting channel; report promptly and specifically; preserve evidence
Find out the reporting mechanism before you need it. When reporting, be specific about what you observed and when. Do not attempt to clean up or investigate independently — preserve evidence for the security team.
Incident response follows a sequence: detect, contain, eradicate, recover, notify
Each phase has a specific purpose. Notification obligations may require action within 72 hours of discovery. Participating in post-incident review contributes to improvements that prevent future incidents.
