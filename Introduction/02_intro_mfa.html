— What Is MFA
Why a password alone is no longer enough
Authentication verifies that a person is who they claim to be
When you log in to a system, you are making a claim: 'I am the person authorized to use this account.' Authentication is the process by which a system verifies that claim. Historically, the primary authentication method has been a password — a secret that only the authorized person is supposed to know. Passwords have served this function for decades, but the threat environment has changed dramatically. Attackers now have industrial-scale tools for stealing, guessing, and purchasing passwords, making single-factor authentication increasingly inadequate as a standalone protection.
MFA requires two or more independent factors to verify identity
Multifactor authentication requires users to present two or more independent forms of verification before access is granted. The three categories of factors are: something you know (a password or PIN), something you have (a phone, hardware token, or smart card), and something you are (a fingerprint, face scan, or other biometric). Requiring factors from two different categories means that an attacker who obtains one factor — for example, a stolen password — still cannot log in without the second. The independence of the factors is what makes MFA effective. Two factors from the same category offer much weaker protection.
Password theft is a large-scale, industrial problem
Billions of username and password combinations have been stolen from breached services and are now available for purchase on criminal marketplaces. Attackers use automated tools to try these combinations against banking sites, email services, and business applications — a technique called credential stuffing. Separately, phishing campaigns harvest fresh credentials directly from users who enter them into convincing fake login pages. Password spraying tools test the most common passwords against large numbers of accounts. The scale and automation of credential theft means that a password, no matter how strong, can be compromised without the account holder ever knowing.
MFA eliminates most credential-based attacks with a single control
When MFA is active, a stolen password becomes significantly less useful to an attacker. Without the second factor — the phone that receives the verification code, the hardware key that generates a one-time password — the password alone cannot grant access. This is why MFA is consistently recommended as the single highest-priority security control by cybersecurity authorities worldwide. Studies have found that enabling MFA on accounts blocks more than 99 percent of automated credential-based attack attempts. The protection it provides is disproportionately high relative to the implementation effort.
— Types of MFA
Understanding the different forms of second-factor authentication
SMS text messages: widely used but the weakest MFA form
One-time codes sent via SMS text message are the most commonly encountered form of MFA because they require no additional application or hardware — just a mobile phone. They provide real improvement over password-only authentication. However, SMS codes can be intercepted through SIM swapping attacks, where an attacker convinces a mobile carrier to transfer the victim's phone number to an attacker-controlled SIM card. They can also be defeated by real-time phishing proxies that relay codes from the victim to the attacker. SMS MFA is meaningfully better than no MFA, but it is the least secure form.
Authenticator apps: stronger, phishing-resistant time-based codes
Authenticator applications — such as Microsoft Authenticator, Google Authenticator, or Authy — generate time-based one-time passwords (TOTP) that refresh every 30 seconds. These codes are generated locally on the device and do not pass through a carrier network, eliminating the SIM swapping risk. App-based authentication is significantly more secure than SMS codes and is the recommended minimum for business accounts. Setup involves scanning a QR code with the app, after which the app and the service share a secret that allows time-synchronized code generation without any ongoing network dependency.
Push notifications: one-tap approval with important caveats
Some authentication systems send a push notification to the user's registered device, which the user approves or denies with a single tap. Push-based MFA is convenient and generally secure, but it is vulnerable to a specific attack called MFA fatigue or prompt bombing. An attacker who has obtained a valid password repeatedly triggers login attempts, generating a stream of push notifications in the hope that the user will eventually approve one — whether through confusion, irritation, or inattention. Organizations that use push-based MFA should configure number matching or additional context display so that users can verify they initiated the login before approving.
Hardware security keys: the strongest and most phishing-resistant MFA
Hardware security keys — physical devices that plug into a USB port or tap against a phone's NFC reader — implement the FIDO2 and WebAuthn standards. These keys use cryptographic verification that is bound to the specific legitimate website domain, making them immune to phishing. Even if a user is directed to a convincing fake login page, the key will not authenticate against it because the domain does not match. Hardware keys are the strongest form of MFA available. They are the preferred option for privileged administrator accounts and for users who are frequent targets of sophisticated phishing attacks.
— MFA in Practice
How MFA works day to day and where to apply it
The MFA login experience: a brief, predictable second step
In normal use, MFA adds only a few seconds to the login process. After entering a username and password, the system prompts for the second factor. The user opens their authenticator app and enters the current six-digit code, taps 'Approve' on a push notification, or inserts and touches their hardware key. The system verifies the factor and grants access. After this, access is typically maintained for a defined session period — the user is not prompted for MFA on every page or action, only at login. Most users, once accustomed to MFA, report that the additional step quickly becomes routine and unremarkable.
Where to apply MFA: prioritize by risk and access level
MFA should be applied to every account that can be enabled, prioritized by the sensitivity of what that account can access. Business email accounts are among the highest priorities — email is used for password resets, financial communications, and access to other services. Cloud platforms, financial systems, and any application containing customer or employee data all warrant MFA. Administrator and privileged accounts require the strongest form of MFA available, ideally hardware keys. Personal accounts — banking, email, social media — should also have MFA enabled. Any account without MFA is a potential entry point that can be exploited with a stolen or guessed password.
Backup codes and account recovery: preparing for a lost second factor
A concern many users have about MFA is what happens if they lose access to their second factor — the phone is lost, the hardware key is damaged, or the authenticator app is uninstalled. Every MFA system provides a recovery path. Backup codes are single-use codes generated at setup time that allow recovery if the primary second factor is unavailable. These codes should be stored securely — printed and kept in a safe physical location, or stored in an encrypted password manager — not in a file on the same device as the authenticator. Organizations typically have a helpdesk verification process for employees who need account recovery.
Phishing-resistant MFA: understanding why some forms are stronger than others
Not all MFA implementations provide equal protection. SMS codes and app-based TOTP codes can be relayed in real time by a phishing proxy that sits between the user and the legitimate service — the user enters their code thinking they are logging into the real site, while the proxy simultaneously uses that code to log into the real site on the attacker's behalf. Phishing-resistant MFA methods — primarily hardware security keys using FIDO2/WebAuthn — work differently. The authentication is cryptographically bound to the specific legitimate domain, making relay attacks impossible. For the highest-risk accounts, phishing-resistant MFA is the appropriate standard.
— MFA and Your Organization
Policies, responsibilities, and what to do when something seems wrong
MFA enrollment: how and why your organization requires it
Your organization requires MFA because it is one of the most effective single measures for protecting business systems and data. MFA enrollment typically involves receiving an email invitation from IT, downloading an authenticator app (if not already installed), scanning a QR code with the app, and entering the first generated code to confirm setup. For hardware keys, the process involves registering the key against your account. Enrollment should be completed promptly when requested. An account without MFA active is operating at significantly higher risk and can be a source of compromise that affects colleagues, systems, and customers.
Recognizing MFA abuse: the prompt you did not initiate
A push notification asking you to approve a login that you did not initiate is a serious security signal. It means someone has your password and is attempting to use it. Do not approve the notification. If your system uses number matching or displays login context, verify that none of it matches activity you actually performed. Report the incident to your IT or security team immediately — they need to know that your credentials have been compromised and may need to force a password reset and audit recent account activity. A single unrequested MFA prompt is not necessarily a crisis, but it is always worth reporting.
MFA fatigue: what to do if you receive repeated unexpected prompts
MFA fatigue attacks involve repeatedly triggering login attempts against an account in the hope that the user will eventually approve a prompt out of confusion or irritation. If you receive multiple unexpected MFA prompts in a short period, do not approve any of them. Deny each prompt using the 'No, it wasn't me' or 'Deny' option if available. Contact your IT or security team immediately. Change your password from a known-clean device as soon as possible. Repeated unexpected prompts are not a system glitch — they are an active attack. Treating them as a routine annoyance rather than a security signal is how these attacks succeed.
The shared responsibility of strong authentication
MFA is a technical control that organizations deploy, but its effectiveness depends on how individuals use and protect the second factor. Keeping the authenticator app on a secure, PIN-protected device, never sharing codes with anyone who requests them by phone or email, storing backup codes safely, enrolling promptly when required, and reporting unexpected prompts are all individual behaviors that determine whether MFA succeeds as a control. An attacker who can convince a user to share an OTP code, approve a fraudulent push notification, or delay enrollment has effectively bypassed MFA without touching the technology. Awareness of how these attacks work is the complement to the technical control.
— Summary
Key concepts from this module
MFA requires two or more independent factors, making stolen passwords insufficient for access
Authentication factors fall into three categories: something you know, something you have, and something you are. Combining factors from different categories means an attacker who obtains one cannot use it alone.
MFA methods vary in strength, from SMS codes to phishing-resistant hardware keys
SMS is better than nothing but vulnerable to SIM swapping. App-based TOTP is the recommended minimum for business use. Hardware FIDO2 keys are the strongest option for privileged and high-risk accounts.
An unexpected MFA prompt means someone has your password and is trying to use it
Never approve an MFA prompt you did not initiate. Deny it, report it to your security team, and change your password. Repeated unexpected prompts are an active MFA fatigue attack, not a system error.
MFA is one of the highest-impact security controls available and should be enabled everywhere
Enabling MFA blocks more than 99 percent of automated credential attacks. Every business account — email, cloud platforms, financial systems, and privileged access — should have MFA enabled as a baseline.
