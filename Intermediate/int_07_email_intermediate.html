— Email Authentication Protocols
SPF, DKIM, and DMARC mechanics and limitations
SPF: authorizing sending sources and its limitations
Sender Policy Framework (SPF) authorizes which mail servers are permitted to send email on behalf of a domain. A DNS TXT record for the domain lists authorized IP addresses and ranges. When a receiving mail server accepts an inbound email, it queries the sending domain's SPF record and checks whether the sending server's IP is listed. SPF passes or fails based on the envelope sender — the address used in the SMTP MAIL FROM command, which is distinct from the displayed From address. SPF has significant limitations in practice. Forwarding breaks SPF: when a mail server forwards a message, it does so from its own IP, which may not be in the original sender's SPF record. The 10-lookup limit means complex SPF records that include many third-party sending services (marketing tools, helpdesk systems, payroll) frequently exceed the limit, causing failures for legitimate mail. SPF alone stops simple direct domain spoofing but does not prevent display name spoofing or lookalike domains.
DKIM: cryptographic message signing and its security model
DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM) uses asymmetric cryptography to provide a verifiable signature over email content. The sending server signs a defined set of email headers and the message body with a private key; the corresponding public key is published in DNS under a selector record at the domain. Receiving servers retrieve the public key and verify the signature. A valid DKIM signature proves that the signed portion of the message was created by a server holding the private key and has not been modified in transit. DKIM survives forwarding because it is tied to the original message content, not to the sending IP. DKIM vulnerabilities include: key length (1024-bit keys can be factored; 2048-bit is recommended), replay attacks (a valid DKIM-signed email can be re-sent by anyone who receives it), and the fact that DKIM signing of the From address domain is what matters for anti-spoofing — signing only passes if the d= domain in the DKIM signature matches the From header domain under DMARC alignment rules.
DMARC: alignment requirement and enforcement policy
DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance) sits above SPF and DKIM and adds two critical properties: alignment and policy. Alignment requires that the authenticated domain (SPF envelope sender or DKIM d= value) matches the From header domain that the user actually sees. This closes the gap where a message could pass SPF for a completely different domain while displaying a spoofed From address. DMARC policy specifies what to do with messages that fail alignment: p=none (report but deliver), p=quarantine (send to spam), or p=reject (refuse delivery). A domain with p=none has no protection against spoofing in practice — attackers can spoof the domain and messages will be delivered. p=reject is the only configuration that prevents spoofed email from reaching recipients. DMARC also provides aggregate reporting: receiving servers send daily reports of authentication results, allowing senders to identify legitimate mail failing authentication before moving to enforcement.
Email account compromise: inbox rules and UAL forensics
When an attacker compromises an email account, their first actions are typically to establish persistence and gather intelligence. Inbox rules — automatic filters that move, delete, forward, or mark email — are the primary persistence mechanism: an attacker creates a rule that forwards all incoming email to an external address and moves or deletes messages to avoid detection. In Microsoft 365, inbox rule creation is logged in the Unified Audit Log (UAL) as Set-InboxRule events. UAL forensics for compromised account investigations examine: the timeline of rule creation relative to authentication events from unusual locations, the content of any forwarding rules, message deletion patterns that might indicate the attacker removing evidence, and access to specific sensitive mailboxes or mail items. UAL must be enabled proactively — it is not on by default in all licensing tiers and must be configured before a compromise occurs to be useful in investigation.
— Email Evasion and Advanced Threats
How attackers bypass email security controls
HTML evasion techniques that defeat content scanning
Email content scanning inspects message content for malicious indicators. Attackers counter with HTML evasion techniques that render correctly in email clients while defeating automated analysis. Zero-width characters inserted between words in URLs or trigger phrases prevent exact string matching while being invisible to human readers. Image-based emails replace text with a screenshot of text, defeating text extraction and keyword scanning. HTML entity encoding replaces characters with their entity equivalents (malware.com renders as malware.com in the browser but may not match signatures looking for the literal string). CSS display property manipulation hides scanner-visible content inside elements that are not rendered in email clients. Punycode in URLs displays as legitimate domain characters in some contexts while the underlying URL goes to a different destination. Each technique requires specific countermeasures, which is why layered email security platforms address multiple evasion methods simultaneously.
Redirect chains and time-of-click URL analysis
Attackers frequently deploy multi-hop redirect chains to evade URL reputation scanning. The URL in the email body points to a trusted intermediate service — a legitimate document sharing platform, a URL shortener, a QR code generator — which then redirects to the actual phishing page. At delivery time, the email gateway scans the URL and finds a trusted service. By the time the user clicks, the redirect chain leads to the phishing page, which may not have existed at delivery time (a technique called late-stage weaponization). Time-of-click URL analysis — rewriting all URLs in delivered email to route through a proxy that re-evaluates the destination at the moment of click — addresses this by analyzing the final destination when the user actually attempts to visit it. Microsoft Defender's Safe Links and equivalent features in other platforms implement this control; it is one of the highest-value email security investments for organizations facing sophisticated phishing.
Attachment evasion: password-protected archives, ISO files, and HTML smuggling
Email attachment scanning faces inherent limitations when attackers design payloads to resist analysis. Password-protected ZIP or RAR archives cannot be scanned without the password; attackers include the password in the email body, which automated tools typically cannot correlate. ISO and IMG disk image files mount as drives in Windows without requiring extraction, bypassing many archive scanning controls. LNK (Windows shortcut) files execute arbitrary commands when opened. HTML smuggling embeds a payload inside a specially crafted HTML file: when opened in a browser, JavaScript in the HTML file reconstructs the malicious payload from encoded data and triggers a download. The downloaded file appears to originate from the user's local machine rather than the internet, bypassing mark-of-the-web restrictions that would trigger security warnings for files downloaded from the web. Each of these techniques requires sandbox execution to detect rather than static content scanning.
Email-based malware delivery: macro documents and living-off-the-land
Microsoft Office macro-based malware has been a primary delivery vehicle for decades. Macros execute VBA code when the document is opened with content enabled. Modern security defaults in Microsoft 365 block macros from internet-sourced files by default. Attackers have adapted to use alternative execution mechanisms: Excel 4.0 (XLM) macros stored in a hidden format that some security tools do not inspect, XLL add-in files that execute code on load, OneNote attachments that can contain embedded scripts, and MSIX installer packages. Living-off-the-land techniques in email payloads use document content to trigger legitimate Windows tools: a macro that launches PowerShell, certutil downloading a payload, regsvr32 registering a malicious COM object. These techniques use the same executables that administrators use for legitimate work, making them difficult to block without impacting legitimate operations.
— Email Security Gateway Configuration
Deploying and tuning enterprise email security
Microsoft 365 Defender: Safe Links, Safe Attachments, and impersonation protection
Microsoft 365 Defender's Advanced Threat Protection provides the primary email security layer for M365 tenants. Safe Links rewrites all URLs in delivered email to route through Microsoft's analysis proxy; when clicked, the proxy evaluates the destination in real time and blocks access to malicious sites. Safe Attachments detonates attachments in a sandbox environment before delivery, delivering a clean copy if the sandbox analysis completes without malicious indicators, or blocking delivery if malicious behavior is observed. Impersonation protection uses machine learning models trained on the organization's communication patterns to identify emails that impersonate executives or known contacts. Anti-phishing policies should be configured in Standard or Strict preset modes rather than default settings; the default configuration leaves significant protection capabilities disabled. Defender for Office 365 Plan 2 adds attack simulation training, threat explorer, and automated investigation and response capabilities.
Email DLP: detecting sensitive data in outbound mail
Email data loss prevention scans outbound messages for sensitive data patterns and applies policy-defined actions: block, quarantine, encrypt, or notify. DLP pattern libraries include: regular expression matching for structured data formats (credit card numbers, Social Security numbers, account numbers), document fingerprinting that creates signatures from template documents and detects derivatives, and sensitivity label integration that applies DLP rules based on the classification label applied to document attachments. For insurance agencies subject to NY DFS 500, email DLP policy should at minimum scan for NPI patterns in outbound email to unauthorized recipients, requiring encryption when NPI is detected and blocking transmission to personal email addresses. Tuning DLP policies requires balancing false positive rates against data protection objectives — overly aggressive policies that block legitimate business communications create operational disruption that leads to policy exceptions and shadow IT workarounds.
Email archiving and eDiscovery: compliance configuration
Email archiving preserves email records for regulatory compliance, litigation holds, and historical investigation. In Microsoft 365, archiving is configured through Exchange Online archive mailboxes and retention policies. Litigation hold preserves all mailbox content — even deleted items — for a defined period, preventing any modification or deletion of records subject to legal discovery. The critical configuration principle is that archiving and litigation hold must be configured before a dispute arises — retroactive configuration cannot preserve records that have already been deleted. Retention policy configuration determines how long email is retained and what happens when the retention period expires: automatic deletion or move to an archive mailbox. eDiscovery search capabilities allow security and legal teams to search preserved mail content across multiple mailboxes simultaneously, which is essential for BEC investigation, regulatory inquiry response, and insider threat investigation.
Email header forensics: reading the delivery chain
Email headers document the path a message traveled from sender to recipient and contain authentication results, routing decisions, and timing information. Reading headers requires understanding that they are added bottom-to-top: the first server to handle the message adds the bottommost Received header; subsequent hops add headers above. The Received-SPF, DKIM-Signature, and Authentication-Results headers show the outcome of email authentication. The X-Originating-IP or similar vendor-specific headers may expose the actual originating IP even when the visible sender is spoofed. Anomalies that indicate potentially suspicious mail: authentication failures or mismatches, excessive numbers of relay hops, timestamps that are inconsistent with the claimed origin, and IP addresses that geolocate to unexpected regions. Email header forensics is a skill required for phishing triage, BEC investigation, and account compromise timeline reconstruction.
— Incident Response for Email Threats
Responding to phishing campaigns and account compromise
Identifying and scoping active phishing campaigns
When a phishing email is reported, the first priority is determining whether it is an isolated message or an active campaign targeting multiple employees. Microsoft 365 Threat Explorer and equivalent platform tools allow searching for all delivered instances of a message — matching on sender address, subject, URL, or attachment hash — across all mailboxes simultaneously. This search determines how many employees received the message, how many have opened it, and how many have clicked any embedded URLs. The scope assessment drives the response urgency: an isolated message warrants investigation and blocking; a campaign that reached hundreds of employees with multiple clicks requires immediate organization-wide communication, URL blocking, and credential assessment for accounts that clicked. Campaign timing correlation — when were messages delivered, when were clicks recorded — determines how long users may have had active sessions from attacker-established access.
Post-delivery mail retraction and URL blocking
After identifying a phishing campaign, security teams can take post-delivery action to remove messages that have already been delivered. In Microsoft 365, this is done through Threat Explorer's soft delete or hard delete actions, which remove messages from recipient mailboxes centrally without requiring user action. URL blocking through the Tenant Allow/Block List prevents future delivery of messages containing the identified URL and blocks access through Safe Links when users who received earlier messages attempt to click. Domain blocking prevents delivery of future messages from the identified sending domain. These actions are taken simultaneously with user notification when possible — removing a message from a mailbox before notifying the user prevents the notification from triggering curiosity about the removed message. Post-delivery remediation capability depends on having the right Defender licensing tier; organizations should verify this capability before an incident requires it.
Credential assessment after phishing link clicks
When a user clicks a phishing link, the investigation must determine whether credential compromise occurred. The analysis examines: the destination URL (was it a credential harvesting page, a malware download, or a reconnaissance link?), whether the user submitted credentials (authentication logs showing successful login from a new location after the click time indicate likely credential submission), whether any MFA was bypassed (successful authentication without an MFA event indicates MFA was not configured or was bypassed), and what actions were taken in the account afterward (inbox rule creation, mail forwarding, sensitive data access, OAuth application consent). The investigation timeline is critical: if the phishing click was two hours ago and the account shows new inbox rules and mail forwarding established ninety minutes ago, the attacker has had ninety minutes of undetected access to the mailbox contents.
Campaign response and organization-wide communication
Active phishing campaigns targeting the organization require organization-wide communication that enables remaining at-risk employees to recognize and avoid the threat. The communication should be timely (sent while the campaign is still in progress, not after it has concluded), specific (describe exactly what the malicious email looks like — sender, subject, attachment name, or URL pattern), actionable (tell employees what to do if they received or clicked — report immediately, do not submit credentials, contact the helpdesk), and non-blaming (frame the message as a security team alert, not a notification that employees have failed). For regulated organizations under NY DFS 500, the incident response timeline includes regulatory notification requirements if the campaign resulted in material data access or system compromise. Post-campaign analysis should assess whether the technical controls that should have blocked the campaign were properly configured and whether policy or configuration gaps need to be addressed.
