— Phishing Infrastructure and Techniques
How modern phishing campaigns are built and operated
Phishing kits and adversary-in-the-middle proxy frameworks
Modern phishing campaigns use phishing kits — pre-packaged toolkits that automate credential-harvesting site creation. Advanced kits use adversary-in-the-middle proxy frameworks such as Evilginx2, Modlishka, and Muraena, which operate as transparent proxies between the victim and the legitimate service. The victim receives a pixel-perfect clone because they are genuinely interacting with the real service through the proxy. Credentials and session cookies are harvested as they pass through, defeating OTP-based MFA entirely. The resulting session cookie provides authenticated access without credentials or MFA, because the victim already completed authentication. These frameworks are openly available and used in mass campaigns targeting Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace at scale.
Domain spoofing: typosquatting, homoglyphs, and lookalike domains
Phishing infrastructure requires domains that look convincing under casual scrutiny. Typosquatting registers domains with common typing errors: rn365.com uses 'rn' which resembles 'm'. Homoglyph attacks substitute visually identical Unicode characters — the Cyrillic letter a (U+0430) is pixel-identical to the Latin a in most fonts, enabling domains that appear identical to the legitimate brand. Subdomain abuse places the legitimate-appearing string as a subdomain of an attacker-controlled domain: microsoft.com.evil.net looks plausible in a hyperlink. Newly registered lookalike domains are a reliable threat intelligence signal — attackers register campaign infrastructure immediately before deployment, and monitoring certificate transparency logs for these domains can surface phishing infrastructure before campaigns launch.
OSINT for spear phishing: building targeted attack profiles
Spear phishing campaigns begin with open source intelligence. LinkedIn provides organizational hierarchy, job titles, reporting relationships, and email address format conventions. GitHub repositories may expose internal tool names, infrastructure details, and employee usernames. Job postings reveal technology stacks — a posting for a Palo Alto Networks Engineer confirms the firewall vendor; a Salesforce Administrator role confirms the CRM. Corporate press releases announce mergers and executive appointments. Email header analysis from previously leaked messages confirms the organization's email format. The combination enables attackers to construct emails referencing real internal projects, real colleague names, and real business contexts — dramatically increasing victim engagement probability.
Spear phishing construction: anatomy of a high-quality attack email
A sophisticated spear phishing email is built to pass both automated and human scrutiny. The sender display name matches a real colleague or vendor; the sending domain is a convincing recent lookalike. The subject line references a real current project or imminent deadline extracted from OSINT. The body uses correct internal terminology, references shared context, and creates calibrated urgency — pressing but not panicked. The call to action is specific and time-pressured. The link passes visual inspection using a legitimate redirect service or a convincing lookalike domain. Attachments, if used, are file types the target regularly processes. Every element is chosen to minimize friction between the email arriving and the target taking the desired action.
— Business Email Compromise
Financially targeted social engineering attacks
CEO fraud: impersonating executives to authorize fraudulent transfers
CEO fraud impersonates a senior executive to contact a financial employee with wire transfer authority. The pretext involves urgency and secrecy: a confidential acquisition requiring an immediate wire, a legal matter that cannot be discussed through normal channels, a tax obligation due today. The impersonation uses a lookalike domain or display name spoof. The attack exploits the authority gradient — employees with wire transfer authority are conditioned to comply with executive requests, particularly when confidentiality is emphasized to prevent verification through colleagues. FBI reporting indicates CEO fraud generates billions in losses annually, making it one of the highest-impact social engineering attack categories.
Vendor impersonation: intercepting payment workflows
Vendor impersonation targets accounts payable by convincing an organization to redirect a legitimate payment to an attacker-controlled account. The attacker identifies active vendor relationships and pending invoices through a compromised internal mailbox or publicly available business information. They contact accounts payable posing as the vendor, claim banking information has changed, and provide new wire transfer details. Unlike CEO fraud, vendor impersonation poses as an external party whose identity is harder to verify through internal channels. The attack is most effective when timed to coincide with an anticipated payment, making the banking change request contextually plausible. Organizations processing multiple vendor invoices weekly are high-volume targets.
Payroll diversion and account takeover variants
Payroll diversion targets HR and payroll systems by impersonating employees requesting direct deposit changes. The attacker emails HR posing as an employee, claims banking information changed, and provides a new account. The fraud is typically not discovered until the employee reports a missing paycheck. Account takeover BEC — where the attacker has actually compromised a legitimate email account — is the most sophisticated variant because emails originate from the real account. All authentication checks (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) validate correctly. The attacker monitors the compromised mailbox for financial communications, inserts into ongoing payment conversations, and redirects payments without the victim ever seeing a spoofed domain. Behavioral monitoring of account activity is the only effective detection mechanism.
Financial controls that defeat BEC
BEC attacks exploit gaps in financial process controls more than technical security gaps. Out-of-band verification requires confirming any payment instruction change through a separate communication channel: a phone call to a number from a prior verified communication — not a number provided in the email being verified. This single control defeats the majority of CEO fraud and vendor impersonation attacks. Dual authorization requires two separate authorized individuals to approve wire transfers above a defined threshold, preventing single-point manipulation. Callback verification for vendor banking changes requires calling a pre-verified number, not the number supplied in the change request. These procedural disciplines must be maintained consistently — a single exception for an 'urgent situation' is exactly what attackers engineer for.
— Vishing, Smishing, and Physical Techniques
Social engineering beyond email
Vishing and AI voice cloning: telephone-based social engineering
Voice phishing exploits the trust that telephone communication carries and the difficulty of verifying caller identity in real time. Traditional vishing uses caller ID spoofing to display a trusted number while the caller poses as IT support, a bank's fraud line, or a government agency. Modern vishing increasingly uses AI voice cloning: a short audio sample of a known individual — from a conference presentation, a YouTube video, or a voicemail — generates convincing real-time voice synthesis. A cloned voice might impersonate a CFO authorizing an urgent payment, a technical support representative requesting credentials, or a familiar colleague making a sensitive request. The psychological authority of a recognized voice dramatically increases compliance rates compared to text-based attacks, because the trust mechanism that voice recognition triggers bypasses analytical skepticism.
Smishing and mobile-specific attack surfaces
SMS phishing exploits the lower security skepticism users apply to text messages and the limited screen space that makes URL inspection difficult. Mobile browsers display truncated URLs that hide the actual domain; link previews may show a legitimate service name while the actual destination is elsewhere. Smishing campaigns commonly impersonate package delivery services and financial institutions with messages claiming urgent action is required. Malicious QR codes present a distinct mobile attack surface: users cannot read a QR code before scanning it, and a sticker placed over a legitimate QR code in a physical environment redirects users to credential harvesting sites without any visual warning. The combination of smaller screens, abbreviated interfaces, and habituated quick interactions makes mobile users more vulnerable to phishing actions than desktop users in the same scenario.
Physical social engineering techniques
Physical social engineering bypasses technical controls by targeting human behavior in physical spaces. Tailgating exploits social norms against confrontation: an attacker approaching a secured door carrying boxes or dressed as a delivery person is typically admitted by employees who hold the door rather than challenge an apparently legitimate person. Shoulder surfing captures credentials entered in public spaces. USB baiting places malware-loaded drives in parking lots or break rooms, relying on curiosity to deliver a payload when an employee plugs the device into a workstation. Impersonation of IT support, building maintenance, or vendor representatives provides physical access to server rooms and network closets. Physical techniques are often most effective against organizations with strong network security controls, because they route around those controls entirely.
Hybrid multi-channel attacks and escalation chains
Sophisticated social engineering combines multiple channels to build credibility and overcome skepticism. A common pattern: a preliminary email establishes context ('I will be calling you shortly regarding your account'), then the call arrives — the expected contact removes the cold-approach skepticism barrier. Another pattern uses a smishing message claiming account compromise, directs the victim to call a provided number, and connects them to an attacker posing as support staff. Multi-stage attacks invest in early low-stakes interactions that build rapport before the high-stakes request arrives. Recognizing this pattern — preliminary contact establishing authority in preparation for a later sensitive request — is the key defensive awareness skill. Legitimate counterparties generally do not need to coordinate multiple channels specifically to overcome a target's skepticism.
— Detection, Response, and Security Culture
Organizational defenses against social engineering
Technical controls: email security layers and phishing detection
Technical email security provides the first line of defense. DMARC with p=reject prevents domain spoofing by verifying that emails claiming to come from your domain were authorized by your mail infrastructure. Without DMARC enforcement, anyone can send email appearing to come from your domain. Email security gateways scan inbound messages against threat intelligence and sandbox suspicious attachments. Advanced threat protection re-evaluates links at click time rather than delivery time, catching URLs weaponized after delivery. These controls can reduce phishing reaching inboxes by 90% or more. They cannot achieve 100% because novel infrastructure, social engineering without links (CEO fraud requesting a wire by reply), and account takeover BEC all evade content-based filtering.
Phishing simulation programs: building detection skills
Phishing simulation programs send controlled realistic phishing emails to employees to measure susceptibility and build recognition skills. Effective programs vary simulation scenarios across different pretexts — executive urgency, IT notification, HR announcement, vendor payment request — because real campaigns use all of these. Simulations should reflect current real-world campaign quality, not obviously low-quality samples that train employees to look for poor execution rather than sophisticated attacks. Training delivered immediately at the moment of a click failure is more effective than batch reporting. Tracking reporting rates alongside click rates provides a more complete picture: an employee who clicked and then immediately reported has demonstrated the critical response behavior, even though they initially engaged.
Reporting culture: the most valuable organizational defense
The most valuable organizational defense against social engineering is a population of employees who report suspicious activity quickly, without fear of judgment for having engaged with an attack. Every reported phishing email — whether the employee clicked or not — generates threat intelligence that security teams use to identify active campaigns, block infrastructure, and warn other potential targets. Organizations where employees fear reporting because they expect blame suppress this intelligence stream entirely. Security awareness programs must explicitly and repeatedly communicate that reporting is always the right action and that no one is blamed for reporting something that turns out to be legitimate. Recognition of employees who report reinforces reporting as a valued contribution, not a confession of failure.
Measuring program effectiveness beyond click rates
Phishing simulation click rates are the most commonly tracked metric but are a limited measure of actual security posture. Outcome metrics that better reflect resilience include: time between phishing delivery and first report (shorter is better), percentage of recipients who report rather than click or ignore, reduction in phishing-related security incidents over time, and qualitative engagement indicators such as employees proactively asking security questions or reporting suspicious physical access attempts. A program that reduces click rates from 30% to 5% through punitive consequences but produces zero reporters may have worse security outcomes than a program with a 15% click rate and a 40% reporting rate with active employee engagement — because the reporting organization is generating the intelligence stream that protects it.
— Summary
Key concepts from this module
Phishing infrastructure uses proxy frameworks, lookalike domains, and OSINT-driven targeting
Adversary-in-the-middle frameworks defeat OTP MFA by relaying sessions in real time. Domain spoofing uses typosquatting, homoglyphs, and subdomain abuse. Spear phishing uses LinkedIn, GitHub, and job postings to build convincing pretexts. Only FIDO2/WebAuthn defeats proxy-based credential theft.
BEC exploits financial workflows; defenses are procedural, not technical
CEO fraud, vendor impersonation, and payroll diversion target payment authorization. Account takeover BEC passes all authentication checks because it uses a real compromised account. Out-of-band verification, dual authorization, and callback procedures on pre-verified numbers defeat these attacks.
Vishing, smishing, and physical techniques bypass email security entirely
AI voice cloning enables impersonation of known individuals from short audio samples. Mobile phishing exploits truncated URLs and habituated interactions. Physical techniques route around network controls. Multi-channel attacks build constructed credibility across interactions to lower skepticism.
Technical controls reduce volume; reporting culture is the most valuable organizational defense
Email authentication, sandboxing, and time-of-click link evaluation reduce phishing volume but cannot stop all attacks. Phishing simulations build recognition skills when used as learning tools. Reporting rate is a better metric than click rate. Fast reporting generates threat intelligence that protects the whole organization.
