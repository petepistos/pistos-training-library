— Incident Classification and Triage
Determining severity and response priority for security events
Event vs. incident: establishing the threshold for response
Security operations centers process enormous volumes of alerts — millions of log events and thousands of security tool alerts per day in large organizations. The vast majority are false positives or low-risk events that require no escalation. Effective triage requires distinguishing between events (security-relevant observations that do not by themselves require incident response) and incidents (confirmed or highly probable security breaches requiring coordinated response). An event might be: a failed authentication from an unusual location (suspicious but explainable), an antivirus quarantine of a known malware sample (detected and contained), or a firewall block of a port scan (expected and handled automatically). An incident might be: confirmed unauthorized access to a system, evidence of active data exfiltration, or detection of ransomware activity. The incident threshold should be defined in the incident response plan — ambiguity about when to escalate creates both under-response (missing real incidents) and over-response (escalating every alert).
Incident severity classification and response SLAs
Incident severity classification determines the urgency of response and the level of organizational attention the incident receives. A common four-tier classification framework: Critical (P1) — active ransomware, confirmed data breach of sensitive records, complete system unavailability affecting core business functions — requires immediate escalation to senior leadership, all-hands response, and actions within minutes. High (P2) — confirmed unauthorized access, suspected data exfiltration, significant system compromise — requires escalation within one hour and senior security team involvement. Medium (P3) — suspected compromise without confirmation, policy violations affecting a small number of systems — requires investigation within four hours during business hours. Low (P4) — policy violations with no immediate security impact, suspicious but unexplained activity — requires investigation within one business day. Each severity level should have defined response SLAs, required response team members, communication requirements, and escalation triggers if the incident severity increases.
Initial triage: the first 30 minutes
The first thirty minutes of incident response are the highest-impact period for limiting damage. The initial triage analyst must accomplish: confirming that a real security event has occurred (distinguishing true positive from false positive), determining the immediate scope (how many systems and users are affected), assessing whether the threat is still active (is encryption still occurring, is the attacker still present, is exfiltration ongoing), identifying the highest-risk immediate actions (does anything need to be isolated right now to prevent spread), and escalating with sufficient information for the incident commander to make resource allocation decisions. The initial triage does not require complete understanding of what happened — it requires enough understanding to make containment and escalation decisions. Analysis depth grows through the investigation phase; the triage phase prioritizes speed of assessment over completeness.
Evidence of compromise: what initial indicators suggest
Initial indicators that trigger incident triage carry differential diagnostic value. EDR alerts for process injection or LSASS access are high-confidence indicators of attacker tool use. Authentication from a new country followed by inbox rule creation is a high-confidence indicator of account compromise. Large outbound data transfers to cloud storage services are high-confidence indicators of potential exfiltration. Antivirus alerts on common commodity malware may indicate a low-sophistication infection contained by the AV, or may be the first-stage loader for a more sophisticated attack. User reports of encrypted files are a lagging indicator — if ransomware is visible, the dwell period has likely concluded and the response is now damage limitation rather than early detection. Each indicator type suggests a different investigation priority and a different set of initial containment actions based on what threat behavior the indicator represents.
— Containment and Eradication
Stopping active threats and removing attacker presence
Containment strategy: balancing security against operational disruption
Containment decisions involve a fundamental tension between security objectives (stop the threat as completely as possible) and operational objectives (maintain business continuity and minimize service disruption). Aggressive containment — taking systems offline, blocking entire network segments — most effectively stops the threat but may disrupt operations significantly. Targeted containment — isolating specific compromised systems while leaving adjacent systems operational — maintains more business function but may be insufficient if the attacker has broader access than initially known. The containment decision should consider: how confident is the team in the scope assessment (is it safe to isolate only the identified systems, or might the compromise be broader than detected), how critical are the affected systems to business operations, and what is the speed of the threat (active ransomware encrypting files requires immediate aggressive containment; a persistent backdoor that has been present for weeks allows more considered response). Containment decisions should be documented with the rationale, not just the action.
Network isolation and endpoint containment techniques
Multiple containment mechanisms are available with different tradeoffs between completeness and operational impact. EDR-based host isolation cuts an endpoint's network access while maintaining the EDR management channel — the security team retains the ability to run investigation commands, collect additional forensic artifacts, and monitor activity on the isolated host. Network-level isolation through VLAN reconfiguration or firewall rules quarantines a network segment, affecting all systems in that segment regardless of whether each is known to be compromised. Cloud environment isolation revokes IAM credentials or applies restrictive security group rules to prevent compromised cloud workloads from making further API calls or network connections. Credential-based containment resets or revokes credentials for compromised accounts to prevent the attacker from authenticating, even if they retain access to a compromised host. The combination of host isolation, network isolation, and credential revocation provides defense in depth for containment.
Eradication: removing the attacker from the environment
Eradication is the process of completely removing the attacker's presence and access from the environment — all malware, all backdoors, all persistence mechanisms, all compromised accounts, and all access paths the attacker established. Incomplete eradication is the primary cause of re-compromise: if any single persistence mechanism, backdoor account, or compromised credential is missed, the attacker can reestablish access after recovery. Eradication requires: identifying all systems that were compromised (not just the initially detected system), mapping all persistence mechanisms established on each compromised system, removing malware and persistence through either targeted remediation or system reimaging (reimaging is more reliable for eliminating unknown persistence), resetting all credentials that were exposed or may have been harvested, and revoking all sessions and access tokens. For sophisticated attackers with extended dwell periods, completeness of eradication is more important than speed — recovering prematurely and being re-compromised within days is worse than taking additional time for thorough eradication.
System recovery and integrity verification
Recovery restores affected systems to known-good operational states while maintaining security controls to detect re-compromise. Recovery sequence matters: restoring from backup without first completing eradication may reintroduce compromised components. The recommended sequence is: complete eradication of all known attacker presence, restore systems from pre-compromise backups (with verification that the backup timestamp predates the attacker's initial access), apply all security patches that existed at the time of compromise and any released since, restore data from backup, and bring systems back online with enhanced monitoring. Recovery validation confirms that restored systems are operating correctly and that security controls are functioning as expected. Post-recovery monitoring at elevated sensitivity — lower alert thresholds, more frequent log review — detects any signs of re-compromise during the period when the attacker may attempt to regain access.
— Regulatory Notification Requirements
NY DFS and other regulatory obligations during incidents
NY DFS 500.17: cybersecurity incident notification requirements
NY DFS 23 NYCRR 500.17 imposes specific notification obligations on covered entities experiencing cybersecurity events. The regulation requires notification to the Superintendent of Financial Services within 72 hours of determining that a cybersecurity event has occurred that: affects the availability, confidentiality, or integrity of information systems or nonpublic information, requires the covered entity to notify any government body or self-regulatory organization, or meets the covered entity's internal materiality threshold. The 72-hour clock begins at the time of determination that the event is material — not at the time of first detection. This distinction matters: an organization may detect anomalous activity on Day 1, investigate through Day 3, and determine on Day 4 that a material breach occurred — the 72 hours begins on Day 4. The notification must include the date, a description of the event, and the covered entity's response. The regulation does not require that the investigation be complete before notification.
Annual certification and the intersection with incident response
NY DFS 500.17(b) requires covered entities to submit an annual certification of material compliance to the Superintendent by April 15 of each year. This certification covers the prior calendar year's compliance with all sections of the regulation. A cybersecurity incident during the covered period does not automatically prevent certification — what matters is whether the covered entity addressed the incident appropriately, implemented required controls, and has a documented compliance program. An incident that reveals a compliance gap (for example, an intrusion that exploited a vulnerability that should have been patched under a required vulnerability management program) represents a compliance finding that must be addressed, but the certification can acknowledge the gap and describe remediation actions. Organizations should coordinate their incident response documentation with their compliance team to ensure incident response timelines, remediation actions, and control implementations are documented in a form that supports annual certification.
Notifiable event determination: what triggers the obligation
Determining whether an event triggers the 72-hour notification obligation requires applying the materiality threshold to the facts known at the time of determination. Key factors in materiality assessment: was nonpublic information (NPI) accessed or exfiltrated by an unauthorized party, did the event affect the availability of information systems material to the covered entity's operations, was the event caused by a failure of required security controls (indicating a compliance gap as well as a security incident), and does the event require notification to any other regulatory body or law enforcement agency. Common incident types and their typical notification implications: successful ransomware that encrypts systems (likely notifiable — affects availability and potentially NPI), phishing credential compromise with mailbox access (notifiable if NPI was in the accessed mailbox), failed intrusion attempt that was detected and blocked (typically not notifiable if no access to systems or NPI was achieved), accidental data exposure by an employee (notifiable if NPI was exposed to unauthorized parties).
Documentation requirements during incident response
Documentation during incident response serves multiple purposes: it creates the record needed for regulatory notification, supports potential litigation and law enforcement cooperation, enables post-incident review and process improvement, and demonstrates due diligence in the event of regulatory examination. Incident documentation should capture: timeline of events (when was each event first observed, when was each containment or investigative action taken, when were notifications made), decision rationale (why specific containment decisions were made, what information was available at each decision point), actions taken by whom and when, forensic evidence collected and chain of custody, communications with leadership, regulators, law enforcement, and counsel, and ongoing status updates as understanding develops. Documentation created during incident response is potentially discoverable in litigation — security teams should coordinate with legal counsel on documentation practices, particularly for events that may result in regulatory action or civil claims.
— Post-Incident Activities
Learning from incidents to improve security posture
Post-incident review: blameless retrospective analysis
The post-incident review (also called post-mortem or lessons learned) is a structured analysis of the incident conducted after the immediate response is complete. Effective post-incident reviews are blameless — they focus on systemic failures in processes, technologies, and controls rather than individual mistakes. This is not a process of accountability avoidance; it is the recognition that security incidents are almost always the result of multiple contributing factors, and identifying only the individual who made the final error misses the systemic improvements that would prevent recurrence. The review examines: what happened and when (timeline reconstruction), what controls should have detected or prevented the incident and why they did not, what the initial detection method was and how detection time could have been reduced, what containment actions were taken and whether faster or more complete containment was possible, and what systemic changes would reduce the likelihood or impact of a similar incident.
Root cause analysis and systemic improvement
Root cause analysis goes beyond the immediate cause of an incident to identify the underlying systemic factors that created the conditions for the incident. The five whys technique — repeatedly asking 'why' until the underlying cause is identified — reveals that incidents labeled as 'human error' often have systemic causes: the human error occurred because a process was unclear, a tool generated false positives that trained operators to ignore alerts, or a security control that should have prevented the action was not implemented. Root cause findings drive systemic improvements that address the underlying conditions rather than just the immediate causes. Common root cause categories in security incidents: missing or misconfigured security controls, unpatched vulnerabilities in systems that should have been in scope for patch management, inadequate detection capability that allowed the attacker to operate undetected during the dwell period, and process failures in access management that allowed an attacker to use accumulated excessive permissions.
Metrics and key performance indicators for incident response
Measuring incident response effectiveness requires defined metrics that capture the dimensions of performance that matter. Mean time to detect (MTTD) measures the average time between when an incident begins and when it is first identified by the security team — shorter is better. Mean time to respond (MTTR) measures the average time between detection and containment — the window during which the attacker is operating detected but uncontained. Mean time to recover (MTTRec) measures the time from incident detection to full operational recovery. False positive rate measures the fraction of security alerts that do not represent real incidents — high false positive rates reduce analyst effectiveness by creating alert fatigue. Alert-to-incident escalation rate measures what fraction of alerts are escalated to incident response — too high indicates poor alert tuning; too low may indicate missed incidents. These metrics should be tracked over time and reviewed in post-incident reviews as indicators of program health.
Tabletop exercises and incident response testing
Tabletop exercises simulate incident response without actual system impact, walking participants through a realistic incident scenario to test decision-making, communication, notification processes, and role clarity. A well-designed tabletop presents a realistic attack scenario — ransomware detected in the environment, a BEC attempt that may have compromised financial systems, a data breach affecting customer records — and injects injects new information at intervals to test how the team adapts as the situation evolves. Tabletops reveal: gaps in the incident response plan (situations the plan does not address), uncertainty about notification obligations and their timelines, communication gaps between security, legal, compliance, and business leadership, and role ambiguity about who makes what decisions under what circumstances. For insurance agencies subject to NY DFS 500, tabletop exercises are a required element of the incident response program. Annual tabletops covering different scenario types build organizational muscle memory for incident response.
