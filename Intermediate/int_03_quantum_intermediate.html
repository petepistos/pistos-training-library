— Quantum Algorithm Mechanics
How Shor's and Grover's algorithms work and what they break
Shor's algorithm achieves exponential speedup through quantum Fourier transform
Shor's algorithm solves the integer factorization problem — given a large composite number N, find its prime factors — in polynomial time on a quantum computer, compared to exponential time on the best known classical algorithms. The key quantum operation is the Quantum Fourier Transform, which finds the period of a mathematical function exponentially faster than classical methods. The period-finding result is then converted to the prime factors through classical post-processing. For RSA-2048, the best classical factoring algorithm (general number field sieve) would require computational resources exceeding all current computing capacity. A fault-tolerant quantum computer with approximately 4,000 logical qubits running Shor's algorithm would factor RSA-2048 in hours. The same principle applies to the discrete logarithm problem, breaking Diffie-Hellman and elliptic curve cryptography on the same hardware.
Grover's algorithm provides quadratic speedup for unstructured search problems
Grover's algorithm solves unstructured search — finding a specific item in an unsorted list of N items — in O(√N) operations, compared to O(N) for classical brute force. Applied to symmetric key search, this means a quantum computer searches a keyspace of 2^n in approximately 2^(n/2) operations — effectively halving the key length. AES-128 would provide approximately 64 bits of effective security against a quantum adversary; AES-256 would provide approximately 128 bits. This reduction is significant but manageable: the current recommendation is to use AES-256 for symmetric encryption (already widely deployed) and SHA-384 or SHA-512 for hash functions. Grover's speedup is quadratic, not exponential — it does not break symmetric cryptography but rather reduces its effective key length by half.
Current quantum hardware and the gap to cryptographically relevant capability
As of 2025, the leading quantum computing platforms — IBM's Eagle/Heron processors, Google's Sycamore series, IonQ's trapped-ion systems — operate with hundreds of physical qubits. The relationship between physical qubits and the logical qubits needed for cryptographic algorithms is unfavorable: error correction overhead means that achieving one logical qubit requires approximately 1,000 to 10,000 physical qubits, depending on the error correction code and the physical error rate. Factoring RSA-2048 with Shor's algorithm would require roughly 4,000 logical qubits, translating to between 4 million and 40 million physical qubits depending on hardware quality. Current systems are orders of magnitude below this threshold. However, the trajectory of improvements — better error rates, more qubits, improved error correction codes — is being tracked by every major cryptographic standards body and national security agency as the basis for migration timelines.
Which cryptographic algorithms are broken, weakened, or unaffected by quantum computing
The cryptographic impact of quantum computing is algorithm-specific. Asymmetric algorithms based on integer factorization (RSA) and discrete logarithm (Diffie-Hellman, ECDH, ECDSA) are broken by Shor's algorithm — they provide zero security against a cryptographically relevant quantum computer. Symmetric encryption (AES) is weakened by Grover's algorithm but remains usable with doubled key length (AES-256 remains viable). Hash functions (SHA-2, SHA-3) are similarly weakened but viable with increased output length. Symmetric MACs are generally unaffected. Code-based, lattice-based, hash-based, and multivariate cryptographic systems — the basis of NIST's post-quantum standards — are believed to be resistant to both Shor's and Grover's algorithms with current parameter sizes.
— Post-Quantum Cryptographic Standards
NIST's standardized algorithms and their technical foundations
CRYSTALS-Kyber: lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism
CRYSTALS-Kyber (formally ML-KEM under FIPS 203) is the primary NIST-standardized post-quantum key encapsulation mechanism, replacing RSA and Diffie-Hellman for key exchange. Kyber's security is based on the hardness of the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem — a computational problem involving solving a system of equations with intentionally introduced errors in a high-dimensional lattice structure. No efficient classical or quantum algorithm for solving MLWE is known. Kyber is designed to be efficient for implementation: it produces compact key material, has fast key generation and encapsulation operations, and has been extensively analyzed for side-channel vulnerabilities. Kyber is available in three parameter sets (512, 768, 1024) providing approximately 128, 192, and 256 bits of classical security respectively.
CRYSTALS-Dilithium and FALCON: lattice-based digital signatures
CRYSTALS-Dilithium (formally ML-DSA under FIPS 204) is the primary NIST-standardized post-quantum digital signature algorithm, also based on Module Learning With Errors. Dilithium produces larger signatures than RSA or ECDSA but offers strong security guarantees and efficient implementation. FALCON (formally FN-DSA under FIPS 206) is an alternative lattice-based signature scheme based on NTRU lattices, producing significantly smaller signatures than Dilithium at the cost of more complex implementation — particularly for embedded systems and constrained environments. Both are suitable for TLS certificates, code signing, and document authentication. FALCON is preferred where signature size is the dominant constraint; Dilithium is preferred where implementation simplicity and broad platform support matter more.
SPHINCS+: hash-based signatures as a conservative fallback
SPHINCS+ (formally SLH-DSA under FIPS 205) is a stateless hash-based signature scheme whose security relies exclusively on the properties of cryptographic hash functions — which are well-understood and not believed to be affected by quantum computing in any fundamental way. SPHINCS+ signatures are significantly larger than lattice-based alternatives (typically 8-50 KB depending on parameter selection), and signing is computationally slower. However, its security assumptions are the most conservative of the standardized post-quantum algorithms: even if lattice-based cryptography were found to have unexpected weaknesses, SPHINCS+ would remain secure. It is appropriate for long-term signing use cases where signature size and signing speed are less critical than maximum security assurance, such as root certificate authorities and software signing keys for critical infrastructure.
Algorithm agility and hybrid deployment during the transition period
During the transition period before post-quantum algorithms are universally deployed and validated, hybrid cryptography combines classical and post-quantum algorithms in the same handshake: the key exchange uses both X25519 (classical elliptic curve) and Kyber, with the resulting keys combined so that breaking either algorithm alone is insufficient to compromise the session. This hybrid approach is being deployed in TLS 1.3 and SSH by major vendors including Google, Cloudflare, and AWS. It provides protection against the harvest-now-decrypt-later threat while maintaining backward compatibility. The hybrid approach also provides insurance against unexpected weaknesses discovered in the new post-quantum algorithms, since security requires breaking both the classical and post-quantum components simultaneously.
— Organizational Cryptographic Risk Assessment
Identifying and prioritizing what needs to migrate
Cryptographic discovery: building a complete inventory of algorithm usage
The first step in quantum risk assessment is knowing exactly what cryptographic algorithms are in use and where. This is harder than it sounds — cryptography is embedded throughout modern IT infrastructure in ways that are not always visible: TLS certificates on web servers and internal services, SSH keys for server administration, certificates in VPN concentrators and firewalls, public key infrastructure (PKI) roots and intermediates, code signing certificates, email encryption and signing (S/MIME, PGP), database encryption keys, hardware security modules, embedded device firmware signing, and API authentication credentials. Commercial tools — Cryptosense, Venafi, Keyfactor — can scan networks and code repositories to identify cryptographic algorithm usage. Manual assessment is also necessary for custom applications and embedded systems that automated tools may not reach.
Risk prioritization: time sensitivity and data lifetime
Not all cryptographic usage poses equal quantum risk. The relevant variables are: the sensitivity of the data being protected (how much harm results from future exposure), the required confidentiality period (how long the data must remain secret), and the likelihood that quantum computers will become capable before the data's sensitivity expires. A TLS connection protecting a web browsing session has a confidentiality requirement measured in minutes — even if that session's data is captured today and decrypted in ten years, the harm is minimal. A contract encrypted today containing trade secrets that remain valuable for twenty years is at high risk from harvest-now-decrypt-later. A certificate authority's root signing key, used to sign certificates for the next decade, is at extremely high risk if the signed certificates rely on RSA or ECDSA. Risk prioritization should be driven by data sensitivity multiplied by required confidentiality lifetime.
Migration complexity: the different challenges of different cryptographic assets
Different types of cryptographic assets pose different migration challenges. TLS certificates have short validity periods and are managed by commercial PKI — they can typically be replaced with new post-quantum or hybrid certificates through normal renewal processes. SSH keys are typically user-managed and require organizational campaigns to update. VPN and firewall firmware must be updated to support new algorithms — requiring vendor coordination and potentially hardware replacement if the firmware cannot be updated. PKI infrastructure — certificate authority hierarchies — must be rebuilt with post-quantum algorithms, which requires careful coordination to avoid breaking certificate validation chains. Custom application code may have cryptographic algorithm names hardcoded, requiring development work. Hardware security modules may need firmware updates or replacement. Embedded systems with non-updateable firmware are the most challenging category.
Supply chain cryptographic risk and vendor migration planning
Most cryptographic exposure in an organization is not in code the organization wrote but in products and services it procures. The organization's quantum risk profile includes the quantum readiness of every vendor who handles its data or whose software it runs. Cloud service providers, SaaS applications, payment processors, network hardware vendors, and security tool vendors all implement cryptography that will need to migrate. Organizations should include post-quantum migration status in vendor security questionnaires, track vendor roadmaps for algorithm migration, prefer vendors who have published post-quantum migration plans with timelines, and include post-quantum migration requirements in new vendor contracts. The supply chain dimension of quantum risk is often larger than the internal dimension — vendor dependencies often drive the overall migration timeline more than internal development capacity.
— Migration Strategy and Implementation
Executing the transition to post-quantum cryptography
Establishing a cryptographic bill of materials and migration registry
A cryptographic bill of materials (CBOM) is a structured inventory documenting every cryptographic asset in the organization: what algorithm is in use, where it is deployed, what data or communication it protects, when it was last rotated, and its migration status. The CBOM is the operational foundation of the migration program — without it, migration efforts are uncoordinated and assets are inadvertently missed. The CBOM should be maintained as a living document with ownership assigned to specific teams and integration with the organization's broader asset management program. Migration status tracking — not started, in assessment, in development, in testing, migrated — allows program leadership to track progress, identify bottlenecks, and report status to senior management and regulators.
Building crypto agility into new systems and applications
Crypto agility is the design property that allows a system's cryptographic algorithms to be changed without requiring a full system rebuild. In practice, this means: abstracting cryptographic operations behind library interfaces rather than calling specific algorithm implementations directly, using configuration files to specify algorithm parameters rather than hardcoding them, designing key management systems to support multiple algorithm types simultaneously, and documenting algorithm dependencies in architecture design documents. New systems built with crypto agility can be migrated to post-quantum algorithms through configuration changes and library updates rather than architectural redesign. Organizations that are building or purchasing new systems should require crypto agility as a design requirement and include it in software security assessments and vendor evaluations.
Managing the PKI transition: certificate authority migration
Public key infrastructure is the backbone of organizational authentication and encryption: it issues and validates the certificates used for TLS, VPN, code signing, email, and device authentication. Migrating PKI to post-quantum algorithms requires planning at the hierarchy level — new root certificate authorities must be established using post-quantum or hybrid algorithms, subordinate CAs must be issued under the new roots, and end-entity certificates must be re-issued under the new subordinate CAs. This is a multi-year process because root CA certificates have very long validity periods and the new root must be distributed to all relying systems before end-entity certificates under it can be trusted. The transition must be designed to maintain compatibility with systems that cannot yet validate post-quantum signatures, requiring hybrid or dual-certificate approaches during the overlap period.
Regulatory requirements and compliance timelines for post-quantum migration
Post-quantum cryptography migration is moving from a best-practice recommendation to a regulatory requirement in several sectors. The United States government has mandated migration of federal information systems to NIST-approved post-quantum algorithms with a target of 2035 for full migration. CISA and NSA have issued guidance for critical infrastructure sectors. Financial sector regulators are beginning to address quantum risk in supervisory guidance. Insurance carriers are beginning to include quantum readiness in cybersecurity questionnaires. Organizations in regulated industries should treat the NIST PQC standards as the baseline for compliance planning, monitor sector-specific regulatory guidance for additional requirements, and be prepared to demonstrate migration progress in regulatory examinations. Early migration is both a security investment and a compliance positioning advantage.

Key concepts from this module
Shor's algorithm exponentially breaks RSA/DH/ECC; Grover's quadratically weakens symmetric crypto
Shor's uses Quantum Fourier Transform to solve period-finding, achieving polynomial-time factoring. RSA, DH, ECDH, ECDSA are broken outright. Grover's halves effective symmetric key length — AES-256 and SHA-384/512 remain viable with current parameter choices.
NIST's post-quantum standards: Kyber (key exchange), Dilithium/FALCON (signatures), SPHINCS+ (hash-based fallback)
All are believed resistant to Shor's and Grover's algorithms. Hybrid deployment combines classical and post-quantum algorithms during transition, protecting against harvest-now-decrypt-later while maintaining backward compatibility.
Risk assessment prioritizes by data sensitivity × required confidentiality lifetime
Long-lived sensitive data — trade secrets, CA root keys, regulated records — needs migration urgency proportionate to their confidentiality requirements. Cryptographic discovery tools and a CBOM are the operational foundation. Supply chain vendor migration planning is as important as internal migration.
Migration requires crypto agility, PKI hierarchy planning, and regulatory compliance timelines
New systems should be built with algorithm-agnostic interfaces. PKI migration requires new root establishment and multi-year certificate rollover. US federal mandate targets 2035; regulated industries should monitor sector-specific guidance and treat early migration as a compliance advantage.
